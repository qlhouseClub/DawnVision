朋友们，今天的AI翻车故事，可以写进《2026年AI影视事故大全》的第一章。

7月19日，由张凌赫、王楚然主演的S+级民国虐恋剧《这一秒过火》正式开播。S+啊朋友们，什么概念？就是平台最高级别的投资体量，亿级制作费，顶流阵容，暑期档头部种子选手。热度确实直冲暑期档顶流——但不是因为剧情，是因为一分半钟的AI片头。

人物轮廓僵硬、光影充满“塑料感”、动作过渡生硬、面部表情像是从十年前的3D游戏里抠出来的。观众的评价非常一致：“这不是PPT动画吗？”“橙光游戏的过场CG都比这丝滑。”“我奶用剪映自动生成的都比这好看。”开播当晚热搜就爆了，讨论的不是剧情有多虐，而是片头有多敷衍。

制作方扛了三天。7月22日，开播三天后发布致歉声明，承认AI片头效果未达预期，替换为真人实拍片头，并解释初衷是“快速转场交代主角情感前史，埋伏笔”。

问题不在AI，在把AI当“省事工具”

朋友们，这事儿的诊断其实非常清晰，就两个字：偷懒。

AI在影视制作中已经全面渗透了。微短剧、动画、虚拟影像领域甚至已经形成了成熟的工业化范式，AI能把制作周期压缩到3-7天，成本仅为传统真人长剧的几十分之一。但问题在于，S+级长剧的观众期待值和低成本短剧完全不同。观众花了40分钟看你的剧，冲着亿级制作的名头来的，结果你给他们看一个五毛特效级别的AI片头——这不叫创新，这叫糊弄。

更深层的问题是制作方对AI的结构性误判。他们把AI当成了“省事的门面”——片头嘛，一分半钟的过渡，不需要演员档期，不需要布景灯光，用AI生成省钱省时间。但他们没想明白一件事：AI是增效工具，不是降本遮羞布。你在最显眼的位置（片头曲是观众对一部剧的第一印象）放一个最低标准的AI生成内容，等于在告诉观众：我们对这部剧的品质没有追求。

这就像你花1000块去吃米其林，结果开胃菜是超市买的速冻小笼包。你跟我说“这是AI做的，很创新”？消费者不买账的。

给所有想用AI做内容的人提三个醒

第一，观众的眼睛是雪亮的，AI好不好一眼就看出来。不要以为观众不懂技术，他们天天刷短视频看各种AI生成内容，对AI味的辨识度比你想象的高得多。尤其是在高期待值的内容上（S+剧、品牌广告、重要活动），敷衍的AI比不用AI更伤害品牌。

第二，AI的用法是“1个专业人+AI>10个普通人”，不是“0个人+AI=专业成果”。真正用好AI的前提是你自己得懂行——懂镜头语言、懂光影、懂叙事节奏，然后用AI来放大你的专业能力，而不是指望AI从零开始给你变出一个合格的作品。你品你细品，连导演和美术指导都不认真审的AI片头，能好看才怪了。

第三，AI翻车的代价往往比省下的成本大得多。一个AI片头省了几十万制作费，但换来了开播即翻车的口碑灾难——热搜是有了，但不是你想要的那种。《这一秒过火》本来可以凭剧情和演员上热搜，结果全被AI片头抢了风头。这笔账，怎么算都不划算。

今天就槽到这里，明天继续。

Friends, today's AI fail story deserves the first chapter in the “2026 AI Film & TV Disaster Compendium.”

On July 19, the S+ Republican-era melodrama “This Second Too Late” (这一秒过火) starring Zhang Linghe and Wang Churan officially premiered. S+ tier, friends — that means the platform's highest investment bracket, nine-figure production budget, A-list cast, summer tentpole. It did indeed top summer premiere charts — but not because of the plot. Because of a 90-second AI-generated opening credits sequence.

Stiff character outlines, plastic-looking lighting, jarring motion transitions, facial expressions that looked ripped from a decade-old 3D game. Audience consensus was instant: “Is this a PowerPoint animation?” “Even dating sim cutscenes are smoother.” “My grandma's auto-generated CapCut videos look better.” The trending topic that premiere night wasn't about how angsty the plot was — it was about how lazy the credits looked.

The production held out for three days. On July 22, they issued an apology, admitted the AI credits didn't meet expectations, swapped them for live-action footage, and explained the intent was “quick transitions to establish the protagonists' emotional backstory and plant seeds for future plot.”

The Problem Isn't AI — It's Treating AI as a “Cost-Cutting Shortcut”

Friends, the diagnosis here is crystal clear, two words: corner-cutting.

AI has thoroughly permeated film and TV production. In short-form drama, animation, and virtual production, mature industrial paradigms have formed; AI can compress production cycles to 3-7 days at a fraction of traditional live-action costs. But the problem is, audience expectations for an S+ long-form drama are completely different from low-budget short dramas. Viewers are committing 40 minutes an episode, drawn by the promise of nine-figure production values — and you serve them a cut-rate AI opening sequence? That's not innovation; that's phoning it in.

The deeper issue is the production's structural misjudgment of AI. They treated AI as a “shortcut for filler” — it's just an opening, a 90-second transition, no actor scheduling needed, no sets or lighting, AI generation saves money and time. But they missed one crucial point: AI is an amplification tool, not a cost-cutting fig leaf. Putting the minimum-viable AI-generated content in your most visible spot (the opening credits are the audience's first impression) tells viewers: we don't care about this show's quality.

It's like paying $150 for a Michelin-starred meal and getting frozen supermarket dumplings as the amuse-bouche. You tell me “it's AI-made, so innovative”? Consumers don't buy it.

Three Reminders for Anyone Using AI for Content

First, audiences have sharp eyes — they can spot lazy AI instantly. Don't assume viewers don't understand technology; they scroll AI-generated content on short video every day, and their “AI taste” detector is far more sensitive than you think. Especially on high-expectation content (S+ dramas, brand ads, major events), half-assed AI damages your brand worse than no AI at all.

Second, AI works as “1 pro + AI > 10 amateurs,” not “0 people + AI = professional results.” Using AI well requires you to actually know the craft — understand cinematography, lighting, narrative rhythm — then use AI to amplify your expertise, not expect AI to conjure a competent product from scratch. Think about it: if even the director and art director didn't seriously review those AI credits, how could they possibly look good?

Third, the cost of an AI fail is often far bigger than the money you saved. An AI opening saved maybe a few hundred thousand yuan in production costs, but bought a premiere-week reputation disaster — they got their trending topic, just not the kind they wanted. “This Second Too Late” could have been trending for its plot and performances; instead the AI credits hijacked the entire conversation. Do the math — it never adds up.
