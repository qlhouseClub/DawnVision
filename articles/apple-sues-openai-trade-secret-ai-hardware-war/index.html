Apple Drops 41-Page Suit on OpenAI
The Hidden AI Hardware War

41页诉状、400多名前苹果员工、Jony Ive的硬件公司、"带苹果零件去面试"——这不是普通的商业机密案，是AI时代两大科技巨头的第一次正面司法碰撞。

A 41-page complaint, 400+ ex-Apple employees, Jony Ive's hardware company, "bringing Apple parts to interviews" — this isn't just another trade secret case. It's the first direct judicial collision between two tech giants in the AI era.

No.013 2026.07.14 约 10 分钟阅读 ~10 min read

41页。

7月10日，苹果公司向北加州联邦地区法院递交了一份长达41页的诉状，把OpenAI、OpenAI的硬件子公司io Products，以及两名前苹果员工Chang Liu和Tang Yew Tan一并告上法庭。案件编号5:26-cv-07078。

这不是什么小打小闹的员工跳槽纠纷。诉状开篇第一句就没打算客气："本案关乎苹果前员工窃取苹果商业机密，系统性地帮助OpenAI加速其消费级硬件业务。"翻译一下就是：OpenAI你偷了我东西，而且是有组织、有计划、成建制地偷。

更微妙的是被告名单里的那个名字——Jony Ive。这位苹果前首席设计官，在2019年离开苹果后创办了设计公司LoveFrom，而io Products正是OpenAI与LoveFrom的合资企业。换句话说，苹果曾经的设计灵魂人物，现在成了被告方的核心成员。

这起诉讼的分量，怎么高估都不为过。一边是全球市值最高的公司，掌控着消费电子行业最严密的供应链和最成熟的硬件方法论；另一边是当前最炙手可热的AI公司，正在从软件向硬件渗透。两边在法庭上的第一次正面碰撞，预示着AI产业的竞争维度，正在从"谁的模型更强"升级到"谁的硬件生态更牢"。

诉状里的"实锤"：从聊天记录到原型零件

苹果的诉状写得相当不客气，甚至可以说"杀气腾腾"。里面披露的细节，每一个单独拿出来都够写一篇头条。

第一个细节是人才掠夺的规模。苹果指控，OpenAI系统性地挖走了苹果的硬件工程师，总数超过400人。这些人分布在芯片设计、传感器、显示技术、产品设计等多个核心部门。400人是什么概念？相当于把一个中型硬件创业公司的核心团队整建制搬过去了。

如果只是挖人，那还属于硅谷的"正常操作"——毕竟员工有择业自由。但苹果的指控远不止于此。

第二个细节，也是最有冲击力的：带苹果零件去面试。诉状称，被告之一的Chang Liu在面试OpenAI职位时，随身携带了苹果未发布产品的原型零件和内部工程样品，用来展示自己的技术能力。你没看错——不是PPT，不是设计图，是实体零件。

这个细节的杀伤力在于：它把"商业机密盗窃"从抽象的知识产权概念，变成了一个非常具体、非常有画面感的行为。你面试的时候把前东家还没发布的产品零件揣兜里，说"你看我做过这个"——这在任何一家科技公司都是红线中的红线。

第三个细节是聊天记录里的"LOL"。诉状引用了被告员工的内部聊天记录，其中一人在讨论挖苹果员工的策略时，用了"LOL"（大笑）的表情。苹果的律师显然很擅长讲故事——这个"LOL"被放在诉状非常靠前的位置，意在塑造一种"OpenAI对窃取苹果机密毫不在意、甚至以此为乐"的形象。

第四个细节是io Products的成立时间线。苹果指出，Tang Yew Tan在苹果任职期间，就已经开始秘密参与io Products的创建工作，违反了竞业协议和保密义务。也就是说，人还在苹果上班，心思已经在帮OpenAI做硬件了。

这些细节叠加在一起，构成了苹果叙事的完整链条：OpenAI有组织地挖苹果硬件人才→核心员工携带机密信息和原型零件→在苹果在职期间就秘密筹备竞品公司→系统性地加速OpenAI的硬件业务。

"400多名前苹果员工、实体原型零件、在职期间秘密筹备竞品——这已经不是普通的人才流动，这是成建制的技术转移。"—— Dawn Vision编辑部

为什么是现在？OpenAI的硬件野心与苹果的焦虑

诉讼选在7月10日这个时间点，非常值得玩味。

就在几天前的7月10日，OpenAI刚刚发布了GPT-5.6系列模型，同步推出了ChatGPT Work企业级智能体产品。这是OpenAI从"对话AI"向"工作AI"战略转型的标志性产品。而就在同一天，苹果把诉状递到了法院。

是巧合吗？可能是。但更可能的是，苹果选在OpenAI风头正劲的时候出手，本身就是一种策略——在你最得意的时候给你一记重拳，让市场和用户都看到：你光鲜的产品背后，可能有不干净的东西。

更深层的背景是：OpenAI正在认真做硬件，而这正在触及苹果的核心利益。

OpenAI的硬件野心已经不是秘密。从2024年开始，关于OpenAI硬件项目的传闻就没断过：有说是AI眼镜，有说是家庭智能设备，有说是类似Alexa的AI助手硬件。Jony Ive的加入更是坐实了这件事——如果只是做个普通的智能音箱，犯得着请苹果的灵魂设计师出山吗？

更关键的是，OpenAI的硬件路线和苹果的战略方向高度重合。苹果这些年在AI硬件上押了重注：Apple Watch的健康AI、AirPods的AI音频、Vision Pro的空间计算、iPhone端侧AI——每一条都是苹果的核心赛道。如果OpenAI带着大模型的优势杀入硬件市场，加上Jony Ive的设计能力，那对苹果来说威胁就不是"又一个竞争对手"那么简单了。

苹果的焦虑还来自另一个维度：人才的持续流失。400多名前苹果员工去了OpenAI，这个数字本身就很说明问题。为什么苹果的硬件工程师愿意去OpenAI？因为在那里，他们做的是"AI时代的下一代硬件"，而在苹果，他们可能还在迭代第N代iPhone的细节。对于顶尖工程师来说，"定义一个新品类"的吸引力，远远大于"优化一个成熟产品"。

这才是苹果真正担心的：如果OpenAI成功定义了AI时代的消费硬件品类，而苹果因为自己的AI进展缓慢而掉队，那损失的就不只是市场份额，而是整个生态的主导权。就像当年智能手机颠覆功能机时代一样——不是你做得不够好，而是游戏规则变了。

AI硬件的下一个战场：从"谁模型强"到"谁体验好"

把这起诉讼放到更大的产业背景下看，你会发现一个清晰的趋势：AI产业的竞争重心，正在从软件向硬件迁移。

过去三年，所有人都在比"谁的模型参数更大""谁的benchmark分数更高""谁的推理更便宜"。但到了2026年，大模型能力本身的差异化正在缩小——GPT-5.6、Claude 3.5、Gemini 2.0、DeepSeek V3，这些头部模型在大多数任务上的表现已经拉不开代差。

当模型能力不再是瓶颈，体验就成了新的竞争维度。而体验的载体，就是硬件。

为什么？因为AI最终要服务于人的真实需求，而人的需求是在具体场景里发生的——你在厨房做饭时需要的AI助手，和你在办公桌前写代码时需要的AI助手，和你开车时需要的AI助手，形态完全不同。软件是通用的，但硬件是场景化的。谁能把AI能力装进最合适的硬件形态里，谁就能占领用户的真实生活场景。

这就是为什么OpenAI要做硬件、为什么Google在押注Pixel和Gemini设备、为什么Meta在做AR眼镜、为什么苹果在Vision Pro上砸了这么多钱——大家都在赌：AI时代的计算平台是什么形态？是眼镜？是耳机？是家庭机器人？还是某种我们现在还想象不出来的东西？

在这场赌局里，苹果有自己的优势：几十年的硬件设计经验、成熟的供应链、庞大的用户基数、强大的隐私品牌。但苹果也有自己的软肋：AI模型能力相对落后、组织文化偏保守、在新形态硬件上（比如Vision Pro）还没有证明自己。

而OpenAI的优势在于：最领先的大模型能力、最前沿的AI产品理解、足够高的品牌热度、以及Jony Ive这样的设计教父加持。但OpenAI的劣势也很明显：零硬件经验、没有供应链积累、没有线下渠道、没有用户信任基础（尤其是在隐私和安全方面）。

所以，OpenAI从苹果挖400多个硬件工程师，逻辑就非常清楚了——我缺硬件能力，而你苹果有最好的硬件人才，那我就直接把人挖过来。这是最快、最直接的补课方式。至于这种方式合不合法、会不会吃官司，从OpenAI的角度看，可能是"先做了再说，大不了赔钱"——只要能抢在别人之前把AI硬件做出来，那点诉讼费根本不算什么。

终局判断：AI竞争进入"全栈对抗"时代

苹果诉OpenAI这起案件，最终的判决结果可能要等好几年。但它的真正意义，不在于谁输谁赢，而在于它标志着一个时代的开始。

第一个信号：AI公司的竞争，已经从"模型层"进入"全栈层"。

两年前，AI公司的竞争还是比谁的模型更强、谁的API更便宜。现在，竞争维度已经全面扩展：模型能力、产品体验、硬件形态、开发者生态、企业服务、安全合规——每一个环节都是战场。OpenAI做硬件、做企业服务、做操作系统；苹果做大模型、做端侧AI、做空间计算——大家都在往对方的地盘里渗透。

未来的AI巨头，不会是只做模型的公司，也不会是只做硬件的公司，而是"模型+芯片+硬件+软件+服务"全栈打通的公司。在这个维度上，苹果和OpenAI的碰撞，只是一个开始。

第二个信号：人才流动的法律边界，会在AI时代被重新定义。

硅谷的传统是"人才自由流动"——工程师从A公司跳到B公司，带着经验和方法论，只要不带走具体的代码和文档，一般都没事。但在AI时代，这个边界变得模糊了。一个参与过苹果芯片设计的工程师，他脑子里的知识和经验，算不算"商业机密"？他能不能把这些经验用在新公司的芯片项目上？

更复杂的是AI模型本身。如果一个工程师在苹果用苹果的数据训练了某个模型，然后他去了OpenAI，把类似的方法用在OpenAI的模型上——这算不算侵权？这些问题，目前的法律体系还没有明确答案。苹果诉OpenAI这起案件，很可能会成为一个标杆性的判例，重新划定AI时代人才流动和知识迁移的法律红线。

第三个信号：消费电子的下一个十年，由AI定义。

过去十五年，消费电子行业的主旋律是智能手机。iPhone定义了整个移动互联网时代的硬件形态和交互方式。但智能手机的创新已经明显放缓——每年的新iPhone都是"更亮一点、更快一点、摄像头好一点"，没有本质变化。

AI正在成为消费电子新的驱动力。端侧大模型、AI眼镜、空间计算、家庭机器人、AI可穿戴设备——这些新赛道里，很可能会诞生下一个"iPhone时刻"。苹果和OpenAI在法庭上的碰撞，本质上是两家公司在争夺"AI时代消费电子定义者"的位置。

当然，这起诉讼也充满了未知数。苹果能拿出多少实锤证据？OpenAI会如何抗辩？Jony Ive在其中扮演了什么角色？最终的赔偿金额会是多少？这些问题，可能要等几年后才有答案。

但有一件事是确定的：从这份41页诉状被递交到法院的那一刻起，AI产业的竞争就进入了一个新阶段。不再是创业者之间的小打小闹，而是科技巨头之间的全栈对抗。法庭上的唇枪舌剑，只是这场战争的序幕。

真正的较量，在产品里。在用户手里。在每一台即将到来的AI硬件里。

41 pages.

On July 10, Apple filed a 41-page complaint in the Northern District of California, suing OpenAI, its hardware subsidiary io Products, and two former Apple employees — Chang Liu and Tang Yew Tan. Case number 5:26-cv-07078.

This isn't some petty employee poaching dispute. The very first line of the complaint pulls no punches: "This case is about Apple's former employees stealing Apple's trade secrets to systematically help OpenAI accelerate its consumer hardware business." Translation: OpenAI, you stole from us, and you did it organizationally, deliberately, and at scale.

The most intriguing name on the defendant list? Jony Ive. Apple's former chief design officer, who left the company in 2019 to found design firm LoveFrom, is connected to io Products — the joint hardware venture between OpenAI and LoveFrom. The man who was once Apple's design soul is now a core figure on the defendant's side.

It's hard to overstate the weight of this lawsuit. On one side, the world's most valuable company, controlling the consumer electronics industry's tightest supply chain and most mature hardware methodology. On the other, the hottest AI company on the planet, now pushing from software into hardware. Their first direct courtroom collision signals that AI industry competition is escalating from "who has the better model" to "who has the stronger hardware ecosystem."

The "Receipts" in the Complaint: From Chat Logs to Prototype Parts

Apple's complaint is written with barely concealed aggression. The details disclosed within — each one alone is headline-worthy.

First detail: the scale of talent poaching. Apple alleges that OpenAI has systematically hired away Apple's hardware engineers, totaling more than 400 people. These individuals span core divisions: chip design, sensors, display technology, product design. 400 people — that's essentially the entire core team of a mid-sized hardware startup, moved over lock, stock, and barrel.

If it were just hiring, that'd be "normal" Silicon Valley behavior — employees have the right to change jobs. But Apple's allegations go much further.

Second detail, and the most explosive: bringing Apple parts to job interviews. The complaint claims that defendant Chang Liu, when interviewing for a position at OpenAI, carried prototype parts and internal engineering samples from unreleased Apple products to demonstrate his technical capabilities. Read that again: not PowerPoint slides, not design documents — physical parts.

What makes this detail so damaging is that it transforms "trade secret theft" from an abstract intellectual property concept into something very specific, very vivid, and very damning. Showing up to a job interview with your former employer's unreleased product parts in your pocket, saying "look what I worked on" — that's a red line at any tech company. The reddest of red lines.

Third detail: the "LOL" in chat logs. The complaint quotes internal chat messages among defendant employees, where one person used a "LOL" emoji while discussing strategies for poaching Apple employees. Apple's lawyers clearly know how to tell a story — that "LOL" is placed very early in the complaint, designed to paint a picture of "OpenAI doesn't care about stealing Apple's secrets; they find it funny."

Fourth detail: the io Products timeline. Apple alleges that Tang Yew Tan, while still employed at Apple, was already secretly working on the creation of io Products, violating non-compete and confidentiality obligations. Translation: still on Apple's payroll, already helping OpenAI build competing hardware.

These details stacked together form the complete arc of Apple's narrative: OpenAI systematically poaches Apple hardware talent → key employees bring confidential information and prototype parts → they secretly prepare competing products while still on Apple's payroll → all of this systematically accelerates OpenAI's hardware business.

"400+ ex-Apple employees, physical prototype parts, secretly building a competitor while still on the payroll — this isn't normal talent flow. This is technology transfer at scale."— The Dawn Vision Editorial Desk

Why Now? OpenAI's Hardware Ambitions and Apple's Anxiety

The timing of the lawsuit — July 10 — is very much worth examining.

Just days earlier, on July 10, OpenAI had just released its GPT-5.6 model family and launched ChatGPT Work, its enterprise-level agent product. It was a landmark moment in OpenAI's strategic pivot from "conversational AI" to "work AI." And on that very same day, Apple filed its complaint.

Coincidence? Maybe. But it's more likely that Apple chose to strike while OpenAI was riding high — a deliberate strategy to land a punch at your opponent's moment of maximum glory, so that the market and users both see: behind your shiny product, there might be something dirty.

The deeper context: OpenAI is serious about hardware, and it's touching Apple's core interests.

OpenAI's hardware ambitions are no secret. Since 2024, rumors about OpenAI hardware projects have never stopped — AI glasses, home smart devices, an Alexa-like AI assistant hardware. Jony Ive's involvement confirms it: if this were just another smart speaker, you don't bring in Apple's design godfather for that.

More critically, OpenAI's hardware roadmap and Apple's strategic direction overlap heavily. Apple has bet big on AI hardware in recent years: health AI on Apple Watch, AI audio on AirPods, spatial computing with Vision Pro, on-device AI on iPhone — each one a core Apple battleground. If OpenAI enters the hardware market with its LLM advantages plus Jony Ive's design capabilities, the threat to Apple isn't just "another competitor." It's existential.

Apple's anxiety comes from another dimension too: continuing talent erosion. 400+ former Apple employees going to OpenAI — that number speaks for itself. Why are Apple's hardware engineers willing to go to OpenAI? Because there, they're building "the next generation of AI-era hardware," whereas at Apple, they might be iterating on the Nth generation of iPhone details. For top engineers, the appeal of "defining a new category" far outweighs "optimizing a mature product."

That's what Apple truly fears: if OpenAI successfully defines the consumer hardware category of the AI era, and Apple falls behind due to its own slow AI progress, the loss isn't just market share — it's dominion over the entire ecosystem. Just like how smartphones flipped the feature phone era — it's not that you weren't good enough; it's that the rules of the game changed.

The Next AI Hardware Battleground: From "Better Model" to "Better Experience"

Zoom out and place this lawsuit in the broader industry context, and a clear trend emerges: the competitive center of gravity in AI is shifting from software to hardware.

For the past three years, everyone was comparing "who has more parameters," "who scores higher on benchmarks," "who has cheaper inference." But by 2026, the differentiation in raw model capability among top players is narrowing — GPT-5.6, Claude 3.5, Gemini 2.0, DeepSeek V3 — these leading models are no longer generations apart on most tasks.

When model capability is no longer the bottleneck, experience becomes the new competitive dimension. And the carrier of experience is hardware.

Why? Because AI ultimately serves real human needs, and human needs happen in specific contexts — the AI assistant you need while cooking in the kitchen is completely different from the one you need while coding at your desk, which is completely different from the one you need while driving. Software is universal; hardware is contextual. Whoever can package AI capabilities into the right hardware form factor will occupy users' real-life scenarios.

That's why OpenAI is making hardware, why Google is betting on Pixel and Gemini devices, why Meta is building AR glasses, why Apple has poured so much into Vision Pro — everyone is gambling: what will the computing platform of the AI era look like? Glasses? Earbuds? Home robots? Or something we can't even imagine yet?

In this gamble, Apple has its advantages: decades of hardware design experience, a mature supply chain, a massive user base, a strong privacy brand. But Apple also has weaknesses: relatively落后的 AI model capabilities, a conservative organizational culture, and unproven new form factors (looking at you, Vision Pro).

OpenAI's advantages: leading LLM capabilities, cutting-edge AI product understanding, enormous brand heat, and a design godfather like Jony Ive. But OpenAI's disadvantages are equally obvious: zero hardware experience, no supply chain accumulation, no offline channels, no foundation of user trust (especially around privacy and security).

So OpenAI poaching 400+ hardware engineers from Apple makes perfect logical sense — I lack hardware capability, and you have the best hardware talent, so I'll just bring the people over. It's the fastest, most direct way to catch up. Whether it's legal or whether it leads to lawsuits — from OpenAI's perspective, it's probably "do it first, pay later if we have to." If you can build AI hardware before anyone else, those legal fees are rounding error.

Endgame: AI Competition Enters the "Full-Stack Confrontation" Era

The Apple vs. OpenAI case — the final verdict might take years. But its real significance isn't about who wins or loses. It's about what it signals: the beginning of an era.

Signal one: AI company competition has moved from the "model layer" to the "full-stack layer".

Two years ago, AI companies competed on whose model was stronger and whose API was cheaper. Now the competitive dimensions have expanded comprehensively: model capability, product experience, hardware form, developer ecosystem, enterprise services, safety and compliance — every环节 is a battlefield. OpenAI is building hardware, enterprise services, operating systems; Apple is building LLMs, on-device AI, spatial computing — everyone is pushing into everyone else's territory.

The AI giants of the future won't be model-only companies or hardware-only companies. They'll be companies that打通 the full stack: model + chip + hardware + software + services. On this dimension, the collision between Apple and OpenAI is just the beginning.

Signal two: the legal boundaries of talent流动 will be redefined in the AI era.

Silicon Valley's tradition is "free flow of talent" — engineers jump from Company A to Company B, bringing experience and methodology, and as long as they don't take specific code or documents, it's generally fine. But in the AI era, those boundaries are blurring. An engineer who worked on Apple's chip design — is the knowledge and experience in his head "trade secrets"? Can he apply that experience to a new chip project at his new company?

It gets even more complicated with AI models themselves. If an engineer trained a certain model at Apple using Apple's data, then goes to OpenAI and uses similar methods on OpenAI's models — is that infringement? Current legal systems don't have clear answers to these questions. The Apple vs. OpenAI case could well become a landmark precedent, redrawing the legal lines around talent flow and knowledge migration in the AI era.

Signal three: the next decade of consumer electronics will be defined by AI.

For the past fifteen years, the main melody of consumer electronics has been the smartphone. The iPhone defined the hardware form factor and interaction paradigm of the entire mobile internet era. But smartphone innovation has clearly slowed — every new iPhone is "a bit brighter, a bit faster, slightly better camera" — no fundamental changes.

AI is becoming the new driving force of consumer electronics. On-device LLMs, AI glasses, spatial computing, home robots, AI wearables — among these new tracks, the next "iPhone moment" will likely emerge. The courtroom collision between Apple and OpenAI is essentially two companies fighting for the position of "definer of AI-era consumer electronics."

Of course, this lawsuit is full of unknowns. How much hard evidence can Apple produce? How will OpenAI defend itself? What role did Jony Ive actually play? What will the final damages be? These questions might take years to answer.

But one thing is certain: from the moment that 41-page complaint was filed, AI industry competition entered a new phase. No longer skirmishes among startups — this is full-stack confrontation among tech giants. The courtroom rhetoric is just the opening act.

The real fight is in the products. In users' hands. In every AI device that's coming.
