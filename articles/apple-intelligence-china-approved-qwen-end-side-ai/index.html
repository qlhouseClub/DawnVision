苹果AI终于要进中国了，但用的不是苹果自己的模型。

7月15日，网信部门公告显示，Apple Intelligence等7款手机端侧生成式AI服务完成备案。其中最受关注的Apple Intelligence，在中国市场搭载的是阿里巴巴的千问大模型。同一天完成备案的还有：华为小艺AI大模型、OPPO Andes GPT、vivo蓝心端侧大模型、小米澎湃AI、三星盖乐世AI，以及努比亚豆包手机大模型。

7款产品，同日获批。这不是巧合，这是端侧AI监管的正式落地——以后手机里的AI功能，不是你想上就能上，得先过备案这道关。

为什么苹果要用阿里的模型？

很多人第一反应是：苹果这么强，为什么不用自己的模型？

答案很简单：合规要求。中国的生成式AI监管规定，向境内公众提供服务的大模型，必须在中国境内训练、在中国境内部署、数据必须留在中国。苹果的Apple Intelligence核心模型是和Google Gemini合作的，训练数据和部署都在海外——直接拿到中国用，不符合监管要求。

那苹果为什么不自己在中国训一个模型？因为成本和时间都不划算。训练一个能跑在手机端的大模型，需要大量的中文数据、算力和时间。与其从零开始做，不如找一个已经合规、已经成熟的本土合作伙伴，直接接入。

这对阿里来说当然是好事——能成为苹果的中国AI供应商，相当于拿到了一张「顶级客户背书」。以后阿里千问去谈其他客户的时候，可以直接说「苹果都在用」。

但更深层的意义在于：AI时代的全球化，和互联网时代完全不一样了。互联网时代，一个软件全球发行，最多做个本地化翻译。AI时代不行——每个国家都有自己的数据主权要求、内容监管要求、安全审查要求。你想进一个市场，可能就得用一个不同的模型。

端侧备案意味着什么？

7款手机端侧AI同日备案，这个信号比苹果用千问还要重要。

在此之前，大家对AI监管的理解主要集中在「云端大模型」——ChatGPT、文心一言、豆包这些云端服务需要备案。但端侧AI不一样，模型是跑在你手机本地的，数据不上传服务器——那为什么还要备案？

因为监管的逻辑变了。以前监管看的是「数据存哪」，现在监管看的是「内容输出是什么」。不管模型在云端还是端侧，只要它能生成内容、能和用户交互，就需要纳入监管范围。

这意味着什么？意味着以后你手机里的每一个AI功能——AI相册、AI写作、AI修图、AI语音助手——都需要经过备案审查才能上线。手机厂商不能再像以前那样，随便更新一个系统就塞一堆AI功能进去。每一个功能都得先过审。

对消费者来说，这可能意味着更慢的功能迭代、更少的「黑科技」。但对整个行业来说，这是AI从野蛮生长走向规范化的必经之路。

苹果用千问进中国，只是一个开始。未来会有越来越多的跨国科技公司，为了进入不同国家的市场，和不同的本土AI厂商合作。全球化没有死，但它的形态变了——从「一个产品走天下」变成了「一个框架，多个本地模型」。

明天见。

Apple Intelligence is finally coming to China — but it won't be running Apple's own model.

On July 15, China's cyberspace administration announced that 7 mobile end-side generative AI services, including Apple Intelligence, have completed regulatory filing. The most closely watched one, Apple Intelligence, will run on Alibaba's Qwen large language model for the Chinese market. Also filing on the same day: Huawei Xiaoyi AI, OPPO Andes GPT, vivo Blue Heart End-Side LLM, Xiaomi HyperMind AI, Samsung Galaxy AI, and Nubia Doubao Phone LLM.

Seven products, all approved on the same day. This isn't a coincidence — it's end-side AI regulation officially going live. From now on, AI features in phones can't just be shipped whenever you want — you have to pass the filing process first.

Why Is Apple Using Alibaba's Model?

Many people's first reaction: Apple is so powerful, why not use its own model?

The answer is simple: regulatory requirements. China's generative AI regulations state that LLMs providing services to the Chinese public must be trained in China, deployed in China, and data must stay in China. Apple Intelligence's core model is developed with Google Gemini, with training data and deployment all overseas — bringing it directly to China wouldn't meet regulatory requirements.

Why doesn't Apple just train its own model in China? Because the cost and timeline don't make sense. Training a high-quality end-side LLM requires massive amounts of Chinese data, compute, and time. Rather than building from scratch, it's smarter to find an already-compliant, already-mature local partner and integrate directly.

This is obviously great news for Alibaba — becoming Apple's China AI supplier is like getting a "premium customer endorsement." From now on, when Qwen goes after other clients, it can just say "Apple uses us."

But the deeper meaning is this: globalization in the AI era is completely different from the internet era. In the internet era, you launched software globally and at most did some local translation. Not so with AI — every country has its own data sovereignty requirements, content regulations, and security review requirements. To enter a market, you might need a completely different model.

What Does End-Side Filing Mean?

Seven mobile end-side AIs filing on the same day is an even more important signal than Apple using Qwen.

Until now, most people's understanding of AI regulation was focused on "cloud LLMs" — services like ChatGPT, Wenxin Yiyan, and Doubao all need to file. But end-side AI is different — the model runs locally on your phone, data doesn't get uploaded to servers — so why does it need filing?

Because the regulatory logic has shifted. It used to be about "where data is stored"; now it's about "what content is being output." Whether the model is in the cloud or on-device, as long as it generates content and interacts with users, it falls under regulatory scope.

What does this mean? It means every AI feature on your phone — AI photo albums, AI writing, AI photo editing, AI voice assistants — will need regulatory review and filing before it can launch. Phone makers can no longer just stuff a bunch of AI features into a system update like they used to. Every feature has to be reviewed first.

For consumers, this might mean slower feature rollouts and fewer "magic features." But for the industry as a whole, it's the necessary path from wild growth to standardization.

Apple using Qwen to enter China is just the beginning. In the future, more and more global tech companies will partner with different local AI vendors to enter different national markets. Globalization isn't dead — but its form has changed: from "one product for the whole world" to "one framework, many local models."
