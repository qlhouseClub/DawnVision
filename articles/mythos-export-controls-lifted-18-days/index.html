Mythos Blocked 18 Days
AI Blockade Proves a Paper Tiger

6月12日封禁、6月26日部分开放、7月1日全面解禁。美国政府对Anthropic最强模型Mythos的出口管制只维持了18天。原因：亚洲AI公司的模型快速逼近，封锁反而让美国AI失去全球市场。

Blocked June 12, partially opened June 26, fully lifted July 1. The US government's export controls on Anthropic's strongest model Mythos lasted just 18 days. The reason: Asian AI companies' models are rapidly closing in, and the blockade was causing American AI to lose global market share.

No.006 2026.07.01 约 5 分钟阅读 ~5 min read

18天。

这是美国政府对Anthropic最强模型Mythos 5的出口管制持续的时间。6月12日援引“国家安全权限”发出管制令，Anthropic被迫关闭全球公开访问；6月26日商务部通知可向“经白宫批准的客户”开放；7月1日全面解除限制，全球用户恢复访问。

商务部长Howard Lutnick给出的解禁条件是：Anthropic同意“主动检测和应对模型相关安全风险、与美国政府合作制定未来模型发布协议、发现恶意活动及时通报”。但这些承诺Anthropic早在几个月前就已经通过Dario Amodei的公开政策文章主动做出了。换句话说，政府解禁的条件，Anthropic本来就在做。

为什么18天就软了

18天就解禁，核心原因只有一个：封锁不住。

就在Mythos被封禁的这18天里，亚洲AI公司的模型在快速逼近。日本Sakana AI发布了Fugu模型，中国的Z.ai（智谱）发布了开源权重的GLM-5.2——The Verge报道，一些研究人员声称GLM-5.2在某些漏洞发现和网络安全场景中已经能匹配Mythos的水平。虽然在通用任务上GLM仍落后于Anthropic和OpenAI的模型，但“中国大幅缩小了差距”是不争的事实。

这就是出口管制的根本悖论：你封禁最强模型，以为能保持技术优势，但开源模型和国际竞争者会快速填补真空，你的封禁反而把全球市场让给了对手。网络安全专家一开始就对这轮管制持怀疑态度——在他们看来，这更像是一种政治施压手段（Anthropic高管曾公开批评政府对AI技术的使用方式），而非真正的安全措施。

Anthropic与加州州长Newsom达成协议，加州政府可以半价使用Claude——这进一步说明，在联邦政府把Anthropic当靶子的同时，州政府在积极拥抱AI。美国AI政策的混乱和分裂可见一斑。

AI政策进入“摇摆期”

Mythos 18天解禁不是孤例，它暴露了特朗普政府AI政策的深层矛盾。

一方面，政府对前沿模型保持警惕——OpenAI的GPT-5.6 Sol也被要求“受控发布”，不是面向所有用户开放，而是先给“经批准的组织”使用；6月发布的一项行政命令要求模型发布前接受审查。另一方面，美国又不能真的封锁自己最有竞争力的技术——AI是美国对华科技竞争的核心筹码，自废武功不符合战略利益。

这种摇摆给整个行业带来了巨大的不确定性。Dean W. Ball（刚加入OpenAI政策团队的分析师）批评了6月的行政命令，认为审查机制不清晰。企业不知道下一个模型发布会不会被卡，投资者不知道估值里要不要打“政策折扣”，国际客户不知道该信任美国供应商还是扶持本土替代。

"特朗普政府飘忽不定的AI决策，让整个行业对未来模型发布的规则毫无头绪。" —— TechCrunch 记者 Tim Fernholz

更深远的影响在于：出口管制的“18天闹剧”向全世界传递了一个信号——美国的AI供应是不可靠的。这会加速各国（尤其是欧盟、中国、中东）发展本土AI能力的决心，也会让国际客户在选择AI供应商时更倾向于“多供应商策略”，而不是深度绑定美国公司。

Mythos解禁了，但AI地缘政治的潘多拉魔盒已经打开。下一个被管制的会是谁？管制会持续多久？没有人知道。唯一确定的是：在AI这个领域，技术迭代的速度永远快于政策制定的速度，试图用传统出口管制框架去约束一个月迭代一次的技术，注定是一场打不赢的追赶战。

明天见。

18 days.

That's how long US government export controls on Anthropic's strongest model, Mythos 5, lasted. On June 12, citing "national security authorities," a control order was issued and Anthropic was forced to shut down global public access; on June 26, the Commerce Department notified that access could be opened to "White House-approved customers"; on July 1, restrictions were fully lifted and global access was restored.

Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick's conditions for lifting controls: Anthropic agreed to "proactively detect and respond to model-related safety risks, cooperate with the US government on future model release protocols, and promptly report malicious activity." But Anthropic had already proactively made these commitments months ago through Dario Amodei's public policy writings. In other words, the conditions the government set for lifting controls were things Anthropic was already doing.

Why They Caved After 18 Days

The core reason controls were lifted after just 18 days is simple: the blockade didn't work.

During those 18 days Mythos was blocked, Asian AI companies' models were rapidly closing in. Japan's Sakana AI released the Fugu model; China's Z.ai (Zhipu) released open-weight GLM-5.2 -- The Verge reported that some researchers claimed GLM-5.2 could already match Mythos levels in certain vulnerability discovery and cybersecurity scenarios. While GLM still trails Anthropic and OpenAI models on general tasks, it's an undeniable fact that "China has significantly narrowed the gap."

This is the fundamental paradox of export controls: you block the strongest model thinking you'll maintain technological superiority, but open-source models and international competitors quickly fill the vacuum, and your blockade instead cedes the global market to rivals. Cybersecurity experts were skeptical of these controls from the start -- in their view, this looked more like political pressure (Anthropic executives had publicly criticized the government's approach to AI technology) than genuine security measures.

Anthropic reached an agreement with California Governor Newsom for the state government to use Claude at half price -- further illustrating that while the federal government targets Anthropic, state governments are actively embracing AI. The chaos and fragmentation of US AI policy is plain to see.

AI Policy Enters a "Wobble Period"

Mythos's 18-day restriction-and-lift isn't an isolated case; it exposes deep contradictions in the Trump administration's AI policy.

On one hand, the government remains wary of frontier models -- OpenAI's GPT-5.6 Sol was also required to have a "controlled release," not open to all users but first to "approved organizations"; an executive order in June required pre-release review of models. On the other hand, the US can't truly block its most competitive technology -- AI is America's core chip in the tech competition with China, and handicapping its own industry doesn't serve strategic interests.

This whiplash creates enormous uncertainty for the entire industry. Dean W. Ball (an analyst who recently joined OpenAI's policy team) criticized the June executive order, arguing the review mechanism is unclear. Companies don't know if their next model release will get blocked; investors don't know whether to apply a "policy discount" to valuations; international customers don't know whether to trust American suppliers or nurture domestic alternatives.

"The Trump administration's erratic AI decisions leave the entire industry clueless about the rules for future model releases." -- Tim Fernholz, TechCrunch Reporter

The more far-reaching impact: the "18-day fiasco" of export controls sent a signal to the world -- American AI supply is unreliable. This will accelerate determination across countries (especially the EU, China, and the Middle East) to develop domestic AI capabilities, and will make international customers more inclined toward "multi-vendor strategies" rather than deep lock-in with American companies.

Mythos is unblocked, but the Pandora's box of AI geopolitics has been opened. Who will be the next to face controls? How long will restrictions last? Nobody knows. The only certainty: in AI, technology iterates faster than policy can be written, and trying to constrain a technology that updates monthly with traditional export control frameworks is a losing race from the start.
