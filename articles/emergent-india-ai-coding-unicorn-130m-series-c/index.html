1年零3个月。从成立到独角兽。

7月15日，印度AI编程初创公司Emergent宣布完成1.3亿美元C轮融资，估值突破10亿美元，正式跻身独角兽行列。这家公司成立于2025年4月——算下来，从0到独角兽只用了15个月。

更夸张的是增长速度。Emergent的年化收入（ARR）已经达到1.2亿美元，付费用户超过20万。这个增长曲线，在SaaS历史上都排得上号——比早期的Notion、Figma、Canva都快。

这家公司你可能没听过，但它正在用「印度速度」重新定义AI编程工具的增长曲线。

Emergent为什么能跑这么快？

Emergent做的产品其实不复杂——就是一个AI编程助手，和Cursor、Copilot差不多。它为什么能在巨头环伺的赛道里跑这么快？

第一个原因是价格。Emergent的订阅费是每月10美元，比Cursor的20美元便宜一半，比Copilot的19美元也便宜一截。对很多个人开发者和小团队来说，价格就是硬道理——功能差不多的情况下，谁便宜用谁。

第二个原因是印度市场的「人口红利」。印度有全世界最多的软件工程师——超过500万人，而且每年还在以几十万的速度增长。这些工程师英语好、对价格敏感、对AI工具接受度高。Emergent从印度市场起家，先吃透本土，再向东南亚、中东、拉美扩张——这些都是价格敏感的新兴市场，巨头们看不上，但加起来体量巨大。

第三个原因是产品定位精准。Emergent没有去和巨头拼「最聪明的Agent」「最强的模型」，而是主打「最实用的日常编程助手」——代码补全、bug修复、单元测试生成、代码解释。都是程序员每天要用的功能，做精做快做稳定，就够了。

说白了就是：用更低的价格，服务更多对价格敏感的开发者，做最刚需的功能。听起来很朴素，但就是管用。

AI轻创业的新范式：印度模式 vs 硅谷模式

Emergent的故事，其实是AI时代「轻创业」的一个新样本。

以前的硅谷模式是：找最牛的团队、做最前沿的技术、融最多的钱、烧钱换增长、最后垄断市场。这个模式在AI时代还有没有效？当然有效——OpenAI、Anthropic走的就是这条路。但这条路的门槛太高了，没有几十亿美元你连入场券都拿不到。

Emergent走的是另一条路：不做最前沿的技术，做最落地的产品；不打最贵的市场，打最大众的市场；不烧钱换增长，靠产品力自然增长。这条路的门槛低得多——你不需要自己训大模型，用现成的API就行；你不需要豪华团队，一个精干的小团队就能做；你不需要融太多钱，因为产品本身能赚钱。

这就是AI时代「轻创业」的魅力。底层模型越来越便宜、越来越强，创业公司不需要在模型上投入重金，只要在产品体验、垂直场景、目标用户上做出差异化，就能切到一块不小的市场。

15个月长成独角兽，这个速度以前想都不敢想。但在AI时代，一切都在加速。未来会有更多的「Emergent」出现——不一定在硅谷，不一定在北京，可能在班加罗尔、可能在圣保罗、可能在雅加达。AI把创业的门槛拉低了，也把增长的速度拉高了。

明天见。

1 year and 3 months. From founding to unicorn.

On July 15, Indian AI coding startup Emergent announced a $130 million Series C funding round, pushing its valuation past $1 billion and officially into unicorn territory. The company was founded in April 2025 — that's just 15 months from zero to unicorn.

Even more impressive is the growth rate. Emergent's annual recurring revenue (ARR) has already reached $120 million, with over 200,000 paying customers. This growth curve ranks among the fastest in SaaS history — faster than early Notion, Figma, or Canva.

You might not have heard of this company, but it's redefining the growth curve of AI coding tools with "India speed."

Why Is Emergent Growing So Fast?

What Emergent makes isn't complicated — it's an AI coding assistant, similar to Cursor and Copilot. So how can it grow so fast in a赛道 packed with giants?

First reason: price. Emergent's subscription is $10/month, half of Cursor's $20 and cheaper than Copilot's $19. For many individual developers and small teams, price is the bottom line — if features are comparable, you go with whoever's cheaper.

Second reason: India's "demographic dividend". India has the most software engineers in the world — over 5 million, and growing by hundreds of thousands every year. These engineers speak English, are price-sensitive, and have high adoption rates for AI tools. Emergent started in the Indian market, saturated it first, then expanded to Southeast Asia, the Middle East, and Latin America — all price-sensitive emerging markets that giants overlook, but collectively enormous.

Third reason: precise product positioning. Emergent isn't trying to compete with giants on the "smartest agent" or "strongest model." Instead it focuses on being "the most practical daily coding assistant" — code completion, bug fixes, unit test generation, code explanation. All features programmers use every day. Just make them fast, stable, and polished — that's enough.

Put simply: lower prices, serving more price-sensitive developers, building the most essential features. Sounds basic, but it works.

A New Paradigm for AI Solopreneurship: India Model vs. Silicon Valley Model

The Emergent story is actually a new blueprint for "solopreneurship" in the AI era.

The old Silicon Valley model was: get the best team, build the most cutting-edge technology, raise the most money, burn cash for growth, and eventually monopolize the market. Does this model still work in the AI era? Of course — OpenAI and Anthropic are following exactly this path. But the barrier is too high; you can't even get a ticket without billions of dollars.

Emergent is taking a different path: not building the most cutting-edge technology, but the most practical product; not targeting the most expensive market, but the mass market; not burning money for growth, but growing organically through product-market fit. The barrier to this path is much lower — you don't need to train your own LLM, just use existing APIs; you don't need a luxury team, a lean team can do it; you don't need to raise tons of money, because the product itself makes money.

This is the charm of "solopreneurship" in the AI era. Base models keep getting cheaper and more capable. Startups don't need to pour money into model development — just differentiate on product experience, vertical scenarios, and target users, and you can carve out a decent-sized market.

Reaching unicorn status in 15 months — that speed was unthinkable before. But in the AI era, everything is accelerating. There will be more "Emergents" in the future — not necessarily in Silicon Valley, not necessarily in Beijing, maybe in Bangalore, maybe in São Paulo, maybe in Jakarta. AI has lowered the barriers to entrepreneurship and raised the speed of growth.
