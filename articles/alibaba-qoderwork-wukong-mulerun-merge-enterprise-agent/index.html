阿里整合Agent产品线
B端生产力战事升级

Alibaba Merges Agent Lines
Enterprise Productivity Wars Escalate

以QoderWork为基础整合悟空和MuleRun能力，陈宇森挂帅，推出面向企业生产力场景的AI产品。大厂Agent战争进入产品整合和B端落地阶段。

Consolidating Wukong and MuleRun capabilities on a QoderWork foundation, led by Chen Yusen, to launch an AI product for enterprise productivity scenarios. Big Tech's Agent wars enter the product consolidation and B2B deployment phase.

No.007 2026.07.02 约 5 分钟阅读 ~5 min read

陈宇森。

这个名字最近一次出现在科技新闻里，还是他作为长亭科技创始人被阿里收购后的一系列安全领域布局。7月1日据36氪报道，他将负责阿里新整合的企业级生产力AI产品——以QoderWork为基础，整合悟空、MuleRun的能力，打造一款更强大的面向企业场景的Agent产品。

内部赛马结束，大厂进入产品整合期

过去一年，国内大模型公司和互联网大厂在Agent领域普遍采取了"内部赛马"策略：多个团队同时做不同方向的Agent产品，谁跑出来资源给谁。阿里这次整合传递出一个明确信号：赛马阶段暂告一段落，开始集中资源打B端生产力市场。

阿里方面回应称，QoderWork、悟空、MuleRun现有产品服务未来将无缝升级，所有用户权益不会受影响。三款产品各有侧重：QoderWork偏向代码开发和工程生产力，悟空偏向通用任务处理和个人助理，MuleRun偏向工作流自动化和企业系统集成。整合之后，三款产品的能力会被打通到一个统一的产品框架下。

这不是阿里一家的动作。过去一个月，字节、百度、腾讯都在对内部AI产品线进行不同程度的整合——C端流量战打得差不多了，大家都意识到：真正能带来大规模收入的不是C端聊天机器人的每月20美元订阅费，而是企业市场的生产力改造。

飞书上周也发布了新的AI功能：AI助手直接住在表格里，群里@就能调用，把脏活累活全包了。钉钉、企业微信也在做类似的事情。阿里这次整合，本质上是在为接下来的企业级Agent大战攒弹药。

企业Agent市场的三个关键变量

为什么大厂现在纷纷All in B端生产力Agent？三个原因。

第一，C端付费天花板可见。ChatGPT用了两年时间做到约亿级付费用户，但C端用户的付费意愿和ARPU值是有上限的，而且竞争激烈——用户不需要同时订阅ChatGPT、Claude、Gemini、Kimi、豆包好几个聊天机器人。企业市场则完全不同，一个中型企业一年的AI预算可能就是几百万几千万，而且一旦进入工作流，迁移成本很高，续约率远好于C端。

第二，Agent技术确实成熟到了能干活的程度。一年前的Agent还经常在演示里翻车，现在OpenSquilla们已经能做到自我验证、闭环迭代，飞书的表格Agent已经能帮你处理实际工作。技术成熟度跨过了"玩具"和"工具"的分界线。

第三，资本和国资也在进场。就在阿里宣布整合的同一天，中电科人工智能产业股权基金登记成立，出资额20亿人民币，由中电科、苏州相城金控、央企战新基金等共同出资。国资背景的产业基金入场，意味着AI To B不仅仅是商业行为，还有政策层面的推动。

当然，B端生产力Agent的竞争不会轻松。阿里面对的对手包括：微软Copilot已经和Office 365深度绑定、飞书钉钉在企业协同场景有天然入口优势、垂直SaaS公司（如Salesforce、Notion）也在嵌入AI Agent能力、还有大量创业公司从细分场景切入。

但阿里的优势也很明显：通义千问的模型底座、阿里云的基础设施、钉钉的企业客户基础、以及三款已验证产品的能力积累。陈宇森带队也释放了一个信号——这不是一个边缘化的内部创新项目，而是阿里在AI To B战场上的主力部队。

2026年下半年的企业AI市场，会比C端更加精彩。聊天机器人的战争可能快到终局了，但企业生产力Agent的战争，才刚刚开始。

Chen Yusen.

The last time this name appeared in tech news, it was for a series of security moves after his company Chaitin Tech was acquired by Alibaba. On July 1, 36Kr reported that he will lead Alibaba's newly consolidated enterprise productivity AI product -- building on QoderWork as the foundation, integrating the capabilities of Wukong and MuleRun into a more powerful Agent product for enterprise scenarios.

Internal Horse Racing Ends, Big Tech Enters Product Consolidation

Over the past year, Chinese LLM companies and internet giants have generally adopted an "internal horse racing" strategy in Agents: multiple teams simultaneously building Agent products in different directions, with resources going to whoever pulls ahead. Alibaba's consolidation sends a clear signal: the horse racing phase is winding down; resources are being concentrated on the B2B productivity market.

Alibaba responded that QoderWork, Wukong, and MuleRun's existing products and services will see seamless upgrades, with no impact on user benefits. The three products each have their own focus: QoderWork leans toward code development and engineering productivity, Wukong toward general task handling and personal assistance, MuleRun toward workflow automation and enterprise system integration. After consolidation, the three products' capabilities will be unified under a single product framework.

This isn't just Alibaba. Over the past month, ByteDance, Baidu, and Tencent have all been consolidating their internal AI product lines to varying degrees -- the consumer traffic war has largely played out, and everyone realizes: what generates real large-scale revenue isn't $20/month consumer chatbot subscriptions, but productivity transformation in the enterprise market.

Feishu (Lark) also released new AI features last week: an AI assistant that lives directly in spreadsheets, summonable by @mention in group chats, handling all the dirty work. DingTalk and WeCom are doing similar things. Alibaba's consolidation is essentially stockpiling ammunition for the coming enterprise Agent wars.

Three Key Variables in the Enterprise Agent Market

Why are big tech companies going all-in on B2B productivity Agents now? Three reasons.

First, the consumer monetization ceiling is visible. ChatGPT took two years to reach roughly 100 million paying users, but consumer willingness to pay and ARPU have caps, and competition is fierce -- users don't need simultaneous subscriptions to ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Kimi, and Doubao. The enterprise market is completely different: a mid-size company might have an AI budget of millions or tens of millions RMB per year, and once embedded in workflows, switching costs are high and renewal rates far exceed consumer levels.

Second, Agent technology has genuinely matured to the point of doing real work. A year ago Agents still regularly crashed in demos; now OpenSquilla and its peers can self-verify and iterate in closed loops, and Feishu's spreadsheet Agent can already handle actual work. Technical maturity has crossed the line between "toy" and "tool."

Third, capital and state capital are entering the arena. On the same day Alibaba announced consolidation, the CETC AI Industry Equity Fund was registered with 2 billion RMB in capital, funded by CETC, Suzhou Xiangcheng Financial Holdings, and the Central Enterprise Strategic Emerging Industries Fund. State-backed industrial funds entering the scene means AI B2B isn't just commercial activity -- it's being driven by policy as well.

Of course, competition in B2B productivity Agents won't be easy. Alibaba faces rivals including: Microsoft Copilot already deeply integrated with Office 365, Feishu and DingTalk with natural entry advantages in enterprise collaboration, vertical SaaS companies (like Salesforce, Notion) embedding AI Agent capabilities, and numerous startups attacking niche scenarios.

But Alibaba's advantages are also clear: Qwen's model foundation, Alibaba Cloud's infrastructure, DingTalk's enterprise customer base, and the accumulated capabilities of three validated products. Putting Chen Yusen in charge also sends a signal -- this isn't a marginalized internal innovation project; it's Alibaba's main force on the AI B2B battlefield.

The enterprise AI market in the second half of 2026 will be even more exciting than consumer. The chatbot wars may be approaching endgame, but the enterprise productivity Agent wars have only just begun.
