"视觉具身智能第一股"暗盘破发
无基石无绿鞋振幅41%

"First Visual Embodied AI Stock" Breaks on Gray Market
No Cornerstone, No Greenshoe, 41% Swing

瑞为技术7月7日暗盘收跌16.94%，盘中最高涨15%最低跌26%，振幅高达41%。没有基石投资者也没有绿鞋机制，AI公司二级市场估值回归的信号已经非常明确。

Reconova closed down 16.94% on the July 7 gray market, swinging from +15% to -26% intraday for a 41% trading range. With no cornerstone investors and no greenshoe mechanism, the signal that AI company secondary market valuations are normalizing is unmistakable.

朋友们，今天A股港股的AI吃瓜现场，比任何段子都精彩。

故事的主角叫瑞为技术，港股代码07656.HK，顶着"视觉具身智能第一股"的光环7月8日正式挂牌上市。但在7月7日的暗盘交易中，它已经给所有打新的投资者上了一堂生动的风险教育课：开盘后最高冲到25港元涨15.4%，最低砸到16.07港元跌25.8%，最终收在17.99港元，较发行价21.66港元跌了16.94%，对应市值54.92亿港元。全天振幅高达41.23%，成交额5310万港元。

这过山车坐的，比任何AI生成的过山车视频都刺激。

无基石+无绿鞋，裸奔上市

朋友们，你知道瑞为这次IPO最勇的是什么吗？既没有引入基石投资者，也没有设置绿鞋（超额配股权）机制。

给不熟悉港股的朋友们科普一下。基石投资者（Cornerstone Investors）就是IPO前提前锁定份额、承诺上市后6个月内不卖出的机构投资者。有基石在，相当于给市场一个信号：这些专业机构都认可这个估值，散户可以放心跟。绿鞋机制（Over-allotment Option）则是承销商可以在股票破发时从公司手里额外拿股票来稳定股价——简单说就是有个"官方托盘"。港股IPO如果这两样都没有，就相当于你裸奔上市，股价涨跌全靠市场自由博弈，没有任何缓冲垫。

瑞为这次是真正的"全流通+薄壳"——全球发售2808.70万股，仅占发行后总股本的9%左右，盘子小、筹码松，很容易被资金拉砸。所以我们看到了41%的振幅：早上冲进去想赚一波的追高资金，当天就被套了20%+；暗盘尾盘抄底的人，也不知道明天正式开盘是涨是跌。

瑞为技术是做什么的？官方说法是"视觉具身智能"——简单说就是做计算机视觉和AI感知技术，应用在智慧零售、智能安防、人机交互等场景。这个赛道不能说不好，但它和现在市场上热炒的"大模型""Agent""人形机器人"这些性感概念其实有一定距离。瑞为成立于2012年，是AI计算机视觉赛道的老牌公司，但一直没有真正爆发式增长。

AI IPO的温度变了

瑞为暗盘破发不是孤立事件，它释放了一个明确信号：二级市场对AI公司的估值正在回归理性。

就在两周前，宇树科技104天闪电过审科创板的消息还在刷屏，市场一片"具身智能第一股来了"的欢呼。但宇树还没正式挂牌交易，瑞为已经给这个热情浇了一盆冷水。投资者开始问一些之前不愿意问的问题：这家公司盈利了吗？营收增长怎么样？核心技术壁垒到底有多高？估值和业绩匹配吗？

这和2023-2024年的AI IPO热潮形成了鲜明对比。那时候只要故事讲得好——"做大模型的""做Agent的""做具身智能的"——二级市场就愿意给高估值。现在不一样了，投资者要看真金白银的营收、利润、客户留存、现金流。PPT上的技术再炫酷，如果不能转化为可持续的收入和利润，二级市场不会买单。

更有意思的对比是：36氪同一天还报道了快造科技（Snapmaker）完成10亿元融资，这是近两年消费级3D打印最大单笔融资，而且是因为U1这代产品营收同比增长10倍。真正有产品、有营收、有增长的硬件公司，一级市场还是愿意给钱的；但没有业绩支撑的AI概念公司，二级市场已经开始用脚投票了。

"一级市场看故事，二级市场看财报——AI公司上市的第一天，故事就结束了，考试才刚刚开始。"—— 一位港股打新老手

朋友们，我不是说瑞为技术不好，也不是说它上市就一定跌。但41%的振幅、无基石无绿鞋、暗盘跌17%，这些组合在一起，提醒我们所有对AI投资感兴趣的人：AI的泡沫不是在一级市场，而是在你不假思考打新的那一刻。

今天就槽到这里，明天继续。

Friends, today's AI stock action on A-shares and Hong Kong exchanges was more entertaining than any meme.

The protagonist is Reconova (HK: 07656), billed as the "first visual embodied AI stock," officially listing on July 8. But in the July 7 gray market session, it gave all IPO subscribers a vivid lesson in risk education: after opening, it surged as high as HK$25 (+15.4%), then crashed to HK$16.07 (-25.8%), finally closing at HK$17.99 — down 16.94% from the HK$21.66 offering price, corresponding to a market cap of HK$5.492 billion. Intraday swing reached 41.23% on turnover of HK$53.1 million.

That rollercoaster was more thrilling than any AI-generated rollercoaster video.

No Cornerstone + No Greenshoe = Listing Naked

Friends, you know what's ballsiest about this Reconova IPO? It brought in zero cornerstone investors and had no greenshoe (over-allotment option) mechanism.

A quick primer for those unfamiliar with Hong Kong IPOs. Cornerstone investors are institutions that lock in shares pre-IPO, committing not to sell for six months post-listing. Having cornerstones signals the market: these professional institutions endorse this valuation — retail investors can follow with confidence. The greenshoe mechanism lets underwriters buy additional shares from the company to stabilize the price if it breaks issue — basically an "official price support." When a Hong Kong IPO has neither, it's like streaking onto the market — price movement is entirely driven by free market forces with no cushion whatsoever.

Reconova's offering was truly "full free float + thin shell" — the global offering of 28.087 million shares represented only about 9% of post-offering share capital. A tiny float with loose chips makes it easy for capital to pump and dump. Which is exactly why we saw that 41% swing: the FOMO money that rushed in at the open chasing gains was down 20%+ the same day; bottom-fishers at the gray market close have no idea whether the official opening will go up or down.

What does Reconova actually do? The official line is "visual embodied intelligence" — basically computer vision and AI perception technology applied to smart retail, security, and human-computer interaction. Not a bad sector, but it's at some remove from the sexier concepts the market is hyping: "large models," "Agents," "humanoid robots." Founded in 2012, Reconova is an older computer vision AI company that never saw explosive growth.

The Temperature for AI IPOs Has Changed

Reconova's gray market break isn't an isolated event; it sends a clear signal: secondary market valuations for AI companies are returning to rationality.

Just two weeks ago, the news that Unitree Robotics cleared STAR Market review in a record 104 days was everywhere, with the market cheering "the first embodied AI stock is here." But Unitree hasn't even officially listed yet, and Reconova has already thrown cold water on that enthusiasm. Investors are starting to ask questions they were unwilling to ask before: Is this company profitable? What does revenue growth look like? How deep are the real technical moats? Does this valuation match actual performance?

This stands in stark contrast to the AI IPO frenzy of 2023–2024. Back then, as long as the story was good — "building large models," "building Agents," "building embodied AI" — the secondary market was willing to pay premium valuations. Not anymore. Investors want to see real revenue, profits, customer retention, cash flow. The fanciest technology on PowerPoint won't get secondary market buyers if it doesn't convert to sustainable revenue and earnings.

An even more interesting comparison: 36Kr reported the same day that Snapmaker (consumer 3D printing) closed a 1 billion RMB funding round — the largest consumer 3D printing round in two years — driven by the U1 product delivering 10x year-over-year revenue growth. Hardware companies with real products, real revenue, and real growth are still getting funded in primary markets; but AI concept companies without performance backing are already being voted against by secondary markets with their feet.

"Primary markets buy stories; secondary markets buy financials — the day an AI company lists, the story ends and the exam begins."— A veteran Hong Kong IPO trader

Friends, I'm not saying Reconova is a bad company or that it will definitely drop after listing. But that 41% swing, no cornerstones, no greenshoe, and 17% gray market drop — taken together, they remind everyone interested in AI investing: the AI bubble isn't in primary markets; it's in the moment you mindlessly subscribe to an IPO.

That's all the roasting for today. More tomorrow.
