Doubao & Qwen Kill Consumer Agents
Anthropomorphic AI Regulation Lands

7月15日。

这是中国C端AI用户需要记住的一个日子。7月4日，字节跳动旗下豆包、阿里巴巴旗下通义千问两大头部通用大模型平台，几乎同步推送官方通知：平台内全部用户自定义智能体功能，将于2026年7月15日正式全面下线。所有用户自建的AI角色、专属助手、虚拟陪伴、垂直场景Agent——那些你花了几个月调教prompt、注入知识库、培养"感情"的数字生命体，将在10天后彻底消失。

这不是一次普通的产品迭代。通知发布的时间点高度一致、动作整齐划一，明显不是两家公司独立做出的商业决策。直接导火索是《人工智能拟人化互动服务管理暂行办法》即将在7月15日正式生效——这是中国第一部专门针对AI拟人化服务的监管法规，而豆包和千问上那些可以谈恋爱、可以当"男/女朋友"、可以模拟已故亲人的自定义智能体，正好撞上了监管的枪口。

从"AI伴侣"到监管红线

过去一年，C端AI平台上最火的不是生产力工具，而是各种拟人化AI角色。豆包和千问的智能体商店里，有几百万个用户自建的AI角色：温柔体贴的虚拟恋人、毒舌吐槽的搭子、模拟去世亲人的"数字生命"、甚至是各种打色情擦边球的"性感机器人"。亿邦动力的报道直接点出了监管的核心关切："别想用软色情做智能体的跳板"。

这些拟人化AI角色的问题在哪里？监管层担心的主要有三点。第一是情感依赖和心理健康风险——大量用户（尤其是青少年）对AI伴侣产生深度情感依赖，把虚拟关系当成现实关系的替代品，长期来看可能影响正常社交能力。第二是内容安全和价值观风险——用户自定义智能体几乎没有内容护栏，很容易被调教成输出极端观点、色情内容、甚至违法信息的渠道。第三是伦理边界模糊——模拟已故亲人的"数字复活"、模拟未成年人的AI角色、深度拟人化带来的欺骗性，这些都是现有法律框架没有覆盖的灰色地带。

豆包和千问的公告措辞很谨慎，没有直接提监管，只说是"服务调整"。但动作的一致性已经说明了一切：不是某一家产品做不下去了，而是整个赛道都收到了明确的监管信号。7月15日是deadline，在此之前必须完成整改，而最稳妥的整改方式就是直接关停用户自定义功能——毕竟审核几百万个用户自建的AI角色，成本高到不可想象。

B端也没闲着：阿里禁Claude、美团限豆包、腾讯设token额度

C端监管靴子落地的同时，B端的AI管控也在密集收紧。

7月3日，阿里巴巴内部下发硬性通知：全公司所有员工必须在7月10日前清空办公设备里Anthropic全系列产品。Sonnet、Opus、Fable这些大模型，连同当下最火的Claude Code编程工具，一个不留，全部卸载。给出的理由是"数据安全合规要求"——阿里员工不得将公司代码、内部文档、业务数据输入到境外大模型中，这是红线。

阿里不是第一家这么做的。7月2日有消息称美团内部开始全面限制使用豆包大模型，要求涉及豆包的业务部门自查并迁移至美团自研的LongCat、DeepSeek等模型。而更早之前，美团已经限制过阿里千问的内部使用。

腾讯的做法更精细。据InfoQ报道，腾讯内部正在推行研发token额度管理制度：普通研发人员每月1400元token额度起步，高级开发者和特定岗位可以申请更高额度，有人的月额度甚至过万。这不是简单的"禁用"，而是把AI使用变成了一项可量化、可管控的成本——用多少、花多少钱、ROI是多少，都要算清楚。

这三条新闻放在一起看，你会发现一个清晰的趋势：中国企业对AI的态度，正在从"鼓励所有人随便用"转向"有管控、有成本核算、有安全边界地用"。2025年大家都在喊"AI First""全员AI"，恨不得每个员工一天用8小时ChatGPT；2026年下半年，企业开始算一笔账：员工输入到第三方大模型的数据会不会泄露？每个月的API账单多少钱？AI工具到底提升了多少效率，还是只是让大家看起来很忙？

三重逻辑叠加：AI产业的合规化拐点

为什么这些事情集中发生在7月初？背后是三重逻辑的叠加。

第一重是监管合规逻辑。《人工智能拟人化互动服务管理暂行办法》不是孤立的政策。从《生成式人工智能服务管理暂行办法》到算法备案要求，从深度合成标识到AI拟人化服务专项监管，中国对AI的监管体系正在快速补全。过去那种"先上线再整改"的互联网产品打法，在AI领域行不通了。7月15日是一个明确的时间节点，后面还会有更多细则落地。

第二重是数据安全逻辑。阿里禁Claude不是因为Claude不好用——恰恰相反，Claude Code是当下最好的AI编程工具之一，很多开发者用了就回不去。但对阿里这样的巨头来说，代码是核心资产，把核心代码输入到境外大模型里，风险是不可接受的。这也是为什么美团、腾讯、字节都在大力投入自研模型——不是因为自研模型一定比OpenAI/Anthropic强，而是因为数据必须在自己手里。

"2025年比谁用AI多，2026年比谁管AI严。当一个技术从'酷'变成'基础设施'，管控就是必然的。"—— 一位互联网大厂CTO在内部会上的判断

第三重是成本核算逻辑。腾讯的token额度制度最能说明问题。2025年很多公司给员工发ChatGPT Plus、Claude Pro订阅，觉得这是小钱，能提升效率就行。但到了2026年，随着Agent使用量爆发式增长，token消耗呈指数级上升——一个AI编程工程师一天的token消耗量可能是普通聊天用户的几十上百倍。豆包大模型日均Token调用量达180万亿，即使按30元/百万Token的输出定价算，一天输出成本就高达54亿元（当然这个数字包含了C端免费用户，但企业级调用的成本同样惊人）。当AI账单从每月几万变成每月几百万、几千万，CFO就必须要管了。

终局判断：C端Agent幻想破灭，B端落地进入深水区

很多人对AI的终极幻想是：一个万能的私人Agent，它懂你、陪你、帮你搞定一切——订机票、写邮件、回消息、甚至当你的精神寄托。豆包千问关停自定义智能体，给这个幻想浇了一盆冷水。

不是技术做不到，而是监管和伦理不允许。一个完全开放、没有边界的C端Agent，在可预见的未来不会出现在中国市场。这不是中国特色，全球都在面临同样的问题：欧盟AI法案已经生效，美国也在讨论AI伴侣和拟人化AI的监管框架，韩国前不久刚出台了AI虚拟形象的伦理准则。AI越深入人类生活，监管就越严格，这是技术发展的必然规律。

但这并不意味着Agent没有未来。恰恰相反，Agent的真正机会在B端——在企业的工作流里、在垂直场景里、在有明确边界和权责划分的生产环境里。天工的Skywork Tags给Agent发"工牌"、阿里整合QoderWork做企业生产力Agent、飞书钉钉把Agent嵌入工作群——这些方向没有被监管砍，反而在加速。因为B端Agent有明确的使用者、有清晰的应用场景、有企业作为责任主体，风险可控。

对创业者和开发者来说，这个拐点释放了几个明确信号。第一，不要在C端拟人化AI上赌大的——情感陪伴、虚拟恋人这类方向，监管风险极高，即使做大了也可能一夜归零。第二，做B端AI要优先考虑私有化部署和数据安全——企业客户最关心的不是你的模型有多聪明，而是我的数据会不会出去。第三，帮企业算清楚AI的ROI——2026年下半年开始，"提升效率"这种空话骗不了人了，你得能拿出具体的数字：用了你的工具，省了多少人天、降低了多少成本、提升了多少转化率。

C端的Agent幻想确实提前破灭了，但B端的Agent工业化落地，才刚刚开始。7月15日不是AI的冬天，而是AI从野蛮生长走向成熟的成人礼。

July 15.

That's a date Chinese consumer AI users should remember. On July 4, two leading general-purpose LLM platforms — ByteDance's Doubao and Alibaba's Tongyi Qwen — pushed nearly simultaneous official notices: all user-defined agent features on their platforms will be fully taken down on July 15, 2026. Every user-created AI character, personal assistant, virtual companion, and vertical-scenario Agent — those digital life forms you spent months prompt-engineering, knowledge-injecting, and developing “feelings” for — will vanish in 10 days.

This is no ordinary product iteration. The near-identical timing and uniform action make it obvious these were not independent business decisions. The direct trigger is the impending effectiveness of the Interim Measures for the Management of Anthropomorphic AI Interaction Services on July 15 — China's first regulation specifically targeting anthropomorphic AI services. And those custom agents on Doubao and Qwen that can fall in love, serve as “boyfriends/girlfriends,” or simulate deceased relatives? They walked straight into the regulatory crosshairs.

From “AI Companions” to a Regulatory Red Line

Over the past year, the hottest thing on consumer AI platforms wasn't productivity tools — it was anthropomorphic AI characters. In Doubao and Qwen's agent stores, millions of user-created AI characters flourished: tender virtual lovers, sharp-tongued roasting buddies, “digital afterlives” simulating deceased relatives, and various “sexy robots” skirting soft-porn lines. An Ebrun report bluntly identified the core regulatory concern: “Don't even think about using soft porn as a springboard for agents.”

What's the problem with these anthropomorphic AI characters? Regulators worry about three main issues. First, emotional dependency and mental health risks — large numbers of users (especially adolescents) developing deep emotional reliance on AI companions, substituting virtual relationships for real ones, potentially impairing normal social functioning long-term. Second, content safety and values risks — user-defined agents have almost no content guardrails and can easily be manipulated into channels for outputting extreme views, sexual content, or even illegal information. Third, blurred ethical boundaries — “digital resurrection” simulating deceased relatives, AI characters simulating minors, the deceptive quality of deep anthropomorphism — these are all gray areas uncovered by existing legal frameworks.

Doubao and Qwen's announcements were carefully worded, avoiding direct mention of regulation and citing only “service adjustments.” But the uniformity of action says everything: it's not that one product couldn't make it — the entire sector received a clear regulatory signal. July 15 is the deadline; compliance must be completed before then, and the safest compliance move is simply to shut down user-defined features entirely — after all, auditing millions of user-created AI characters would be prohibitively expensive.

B2B Isn't Sitting Still Either: Alibaba Bans Claude, Meituan Restricts Doubao, Tencent Sets Token Quotas

Even as the consumer regulatory shoe drops, B2B AI governance is tightening rapidly.

On July 3, Alibaba issued a hard internal notice: all employees must remove all Anthropic products from work devices by July 10. Sonnet, Opus, Fable — these LLMs, along with the wildly popular Claude Code programming tool, must all be uninstalled, no exceptions. The stated reason: “data security compliance requirements” — Alibaba employees are forbidden from inputting company code, internal documents, or business data into overseas LLMs. That's a red line.

Alibaba isn't the first. On July 2, reports emerged that Meituan had begun comprehensively restricting internal use of the Doubao LLM, requiring business units using Doubao to self-audit and migrate to Meituan's self-developed LongCat, DeepSeek, and other models. Even earlier, Meituan had already restricted internal use of Alibaba's Qwen.

Tencent took a more nuanced approach. According to InfoQ, Tencent is rolling out a developer token quota system internally: ordinary developers start at 1,400 RMB/month in token quotas; senior developers and specific roles can apply for higher quotas, with some exceeding 10,000 RMB/month. This isn't a simple “ban” — it's turning AI usage into a quantifiable, governable cost: how much you use, how much it costs, what the ROI is — all must be accounted for.

Pull these three stories together and a clear trend emerges: Chinese enterprises are shifting from ‘encourage everyone to use AI freely’ to ‘use AI with governance, cost accounting, and security boundaries’. In 2025 everyone was shouting “AI First” and “AI for all,” eager to have every employee spend 8 hours a day on ChatGPT; by H2 2026, enterprises are doing the math: will the data employees type into third-party LLMs leak? How much are the monthly API bills? Are AI tools actually improving productivity, or just making people look busy?

Three Overlapping Logics: AI Industry's Compliance Inflection Point

Why is all this happening in early July? Three overlapping logics are at work.

The first is regulatory compliance logic. The Interim Measures for Anthropomorphic AI Services aren't an isolated policy. From the Interim Measures for Generative AI Services to algorithm filing requirements, from deep synthesis labeling to special regulation of anthropomorphic AI services, China's AI regulatory system is rapidly filling in. The old internet playbook of “launch first, fix later” doesn't work in AI. July 15 is a clear time marker; more detailed rules will follow.

The second is data security logic. Alibaba didn't ban Claude because Claude isn't good — quite the opposite, Claude Code is one of the best AI programming tools available, and many developers can't go back after using it. But for a giant like Alibaba, code is a core asset, and inputting core code into overseas LLMs carries unacceptable risk. That's why Meituan, Tencent, and ByteDance are all investing heavily in self-developed models — not necessarily because self-developed models are better than OpenAI/Anthropic, but because data must stay in their own hands.

“In 2025 everyone competed on who used AI the most; in 2026 everyone competes on who governs AI the strictest. When a technology goes from ‘cool’ to ‘infrastructure,’ regulation is inevitable.”— A Big Tech CTO’s internal assessment

The third is cost accounting logic. Tencent's token quota system illustrates this best. In 2025 many companies handed out ChatGPT Plus and Claude Pro subscriptions to employees, thinking it was small change that boosted productivity. But by 2026, with Agent usage exploding, token consumption is rising exponentially — an AI coding engineer can burn through tens to hundreds of times more tokens per day than a casual chat user. Doubao processes 180 trillion tokens daily; even at 30 RMB per million output tokens, daily output costs would reach 5.4 billion RMB (this figure includes free consumer users, but enterprise-level call costs are similarly staggering). When AI bills go from tens of thousands per month to millions or tens of millions, the CFO has to step in.

Endgame: The Consumer Agent Dream Dies, B2B Deployment Enters Deep Water

Many people's ultimate AI fantasy was: a universal personal Agent that understands you, keeps you company, handles everything for you — booking flights, writing emails, replying to messages, even serving as your emotional anchor. Doubao and Qwen shutting down custom agents pours cold water on that fantasy.

It's not that technology can't do it; regulation and ethics won't allow it. A completely open, boundary-free consumer Agent won't appear in the Chinese market in the foreseeable future. This isn't unique to China — the world faces the same problem: the EU AI Act is already in effect, the U.S. is debating regulatory frameworks for AI companions and anthropomorphic AI, and South Korea recently issued ethical guidelines for AI avatars. The more deeply AI penetrates human life, the stricter regulation becomes — that's an inevitable law of technological development.

But that doesn't mean Agents have no future. Quite the opposite: the real opportunity for Agents is in B2B — in enterprise workflows, in vertical scenarios, in production environments with clear boundaries and defined accountability. Skywork's Tags giving Agents “employee badges,” Alibaba integrating QoderWork for enterprise productivity Agents, Feishu and DingTalk embedding Agents in work groups — these directions haven't been axed by regulators; they're accelerating. Because B2B Agents have identified users, clear application scenarios, and enterprises as responsible parties — risks are controllable.

For founders and developers, this inflection point sends several clear signals. First, don't bet big on consumer anthropomorphic AI — emotional companionship, virtual lovers, and similar directions carry extreme regulatory risk; even if you scale, you could go to zero overnight. Second, for B2B AI, prioritize private deployment and data security — enterprise clients care less about how smart your model is and more about whether their data stays private. Third, help enterprises calculate AI's ROI — starting in H2 2026, vague claims of “improved efficiency” won't fly; you need concrete numbers: using your tool saved how many person-days, reduced costs by how much, improved conversion by what margin.

The consumer Agent dream may have died early, but industrial-scale B2B Agent deployment is just beginning. July 15 isn't AI's winter — it's AI's coming-of-age ceremony, from wild growth to maturity.
