朋友们，今天这个AI圈乐子，够我笑到周末。

事情是这样的：Meta最近发了一条新广告，主题是"AI乐观主义"。画面拍得特别正能量——黑白镜头里一只眼睛在看关于AI抢工作、AI引发危机的恐慌头条，然后画面一转变成彩色，情侣在屋顶跳舞指着彩虹，青少年在湖里游泳，孩子在田野里奔跑，朋友久别重逢拥抱。旁白深情地说："有些人会让你相信AI会让我们更疏远，会把我们抛下。我们完全不同意。"

你猜这条广告的背景音乐是什么？是David Bowie的《Five Years》。

如果你对这首歌不熟悉，我给你讲讲它讲了什么。新闻传来，地球还有五年就要毁灭了，人类陷入恐慌。歌词开头是这样的："News had just come over / We had five years left to cry in / News guy wept and told us / Earth was really dying"（消息刚刚传来/我们还有五年可以哭泣/新闻主播哭着告诉我们/地球真的要死了）。

是的，你没有看错。Meta选了一首关于人类得知自己只剩五年寿命后集体恐慌的歌，来给"AI让世界更美好"的正能量广告当BGM。

小扎亲自转发，配文"未来属于所有人"

更绝的是，Mark Zuckerberg本人还在Facebook上转发了这条广告，配文写的是："Meta一直相信给人们分享、连接、按你想要的方式塑造世界的力量。当我们进入AI的下一波浪潮，我们继续相信未来属于所有人。"

朋友们，你们品，你们细品。"未来属于所有人"——BGM是"我们还有五年可活"。

TechCrunch的记者Amanda Silberling为了确认自己不是过度解读，专门找了她那个"平时不爱读书、喜欢Claude Code、做区块链分析"的弟弟问这首歌讲什么。她弟弟说："一开始以为是气候变化，然后以为是僵尸末日，然后以为是小行星撞地球。"是的，连一个不读诗的理工男都听出来了，这歌讲的是世界末日。

Meta的广告团队为什么会选这首歌？因为这首歌的副歌部分反复唱"people"（人们/人民），听起来很正能量、很振奋人心。如果不听前面的主歌、不看歌词、不知道这首歌的背景，你可能真的会觉得这是一首歌颂人类团结的歌。但只要稍微做一点功课——哪怕花30秒读一下歌词——都不可能犯这种错误。

给大家几个实用提醒

1. 做营销、做PPT、做发布会选BGM的时候，一定要完整读一遍歌词，不要只听副歌好听就用。Google一下"这首歌讲的是什么"只需要30秒，但翻车了要洗好久。

Friends, the AI industry has delivered a story so funny today that it'll carry me through the weekend.

Here's what happened: Meta recently released a new ad themed "AI optimism." The visuals are aggressively positive — a black-and-white shot of an eye reading panicked headlines about AI taking jobs and sparking crisis, then the world shifts to color: couples dancing on rooftops pointing at rainbows, teens swimming in lakes, kids frolicking in fields, friends embracing after time apart. A voiceover says earnestly: "Some people will have you believe AI is going to make us feel less connected. That it's going to leave us behind. We couldn't disagree more."

Guess what the soundtrack is? David Bowie's "Five Years."

If you don't know the song, let me tell you what it's about. News breaks that the earth has five years left before it dies, and humanity panics. The opening lines: "News had just come over / We had five years left to cry in / News guy wept and told us / Earth was really dying."

Yes, you read that correctly. Meta picked a song about humanity collectively panicking after learning it has five years to live as the BGM for its upbeat "AI makes the world better" ad.

Zuck Himself Shared It, Captioned "The Future Is for Everyone"

Even better: Mark Zuckerberg personally shared the ad on Facebook, writing: "Meta has always believed in giving people the power to share, connect, and shape your world in the ways you want. As we enter this next wave with AI, we continue to believe the future is for everyone."

Friends, let that sink in. "The future is for everyone" — soundtracked by "we've got five years left to cry."

TechCrunch reporter Amanda Silberling, to make sure she wasn't overreading, asked her brother — "a blockchain analyst who loves Claude Code and does not read for fun" — what the song was about. His answer: "I thought climate change at first, then zombie apocalypse, then an asteroid hitting the earth." Yep. Even a STEM bro who doesn't read poetry could tell: this song is about the apocalypse.

Why did Meta's ad team pick this song? Because the chorus repeats the word "people" over and over, sounding upbeat and inspiring. If you don't listen to the verses, don't read the lyrics, and don't know the song's context, you might actually think it's a tune about human unity. But with even 30 seconds of basic research — literally just reading the lyrics — this mistake was impossible to make.

This Isn't the First AI Company Ad Fail Lately

Believe it or not, Meta isn't the only AI company to release a horror-show ad recently.

A few weeks ago, Anthropic released an ad that opens with a burning house, then cuts to a series of still images: crowds surveilled by facial recognition, homeless people sleeping on the street, row after row of tombstones in a cemetery, what appear to be laborers toiling in a mine. The voiceover features different people asking: "Can AI be trusted?" "Who's gonna hit the brakes if we need to?" OpenAI's Sam Altman saw it and roasted it on X: "I thought this was satire, kept looking for the handle to be spelled c1audeai or something."

Palantir — the company that builds AI surveillance systems for governments — is named after the Palantíri, the "seeing stones" in Lord of the Rings that the Dark Lord uses to spy on his enemies. OpenAI CEO Sam Altman has repeatedly cited the movie "Her" as inspiration — "Her" being a film about the tragedy of over-reliance on AI for emotional companionship. Oculus used to give new employees copies of "Ready Player One" — a novel set in a dystopia where a tech company making VR products becomes overly powerful and evil.

"Sci-Fi Author: In my book I invented the Torment Nexus as a cautionary tale. Tech Company: At long last, we have created the Torment Nexus from classic sci-fi novel Don't Create The Torment Nexus!"— A classic Twitter meme by @AlexBlechman

These tech leaders lift names and concepts from dystopian sci-fi, then run ads telling everyone "AI is making the world a better place." The irony is thicker than Meta's BGM choice.

A recent Pew survey found that only 16% of Americans think AI will have a positive impact on society over the next 20 years, with 40% expecting a negative impact. After seeing Meta and Anthropic's ads, I'm starting to understand why — if you can't even pick a non-apocalyptic BGM for your ad, how is anyone supposed to trust you to manage AGI?

A Few Practical Reminders

1. When picking BGM for marketing, decks, or keynotes, always read the full lyrics. Don't just use a song because the chorus sounds catchy. Googling "what is this song about" takes 30 seconds; cleaning up after a PR disaster takes weeks.

2. Before publishing PR content, have a friend with a humanities background glance at it. Tech folks might hear "Five Years" as rousing; anyone who's read a book knows what it's about. Hiring one English major to review marketing copy is way cheaper than an apology tour.

3. If you work in Meta's marketing department, you might want to avoid Twitter/X today. The replies are already a roast session.
