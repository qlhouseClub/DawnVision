欧盟的反垄断大棒，这次精准地砸在了Google的AI命门上。

7月16日，欧盟根据数字市场法（DMA）作出两项最新裁决：Google必须向竞争对手开放Android操作系统和Google搜索的关键部分，允许竞品AI助手和搜索引擎接入。简单说就是：你的手机是Android的，但你可以用别的AI助手，而且Google不能给它使绊子。

这不是普通的罚款——罚款Google交得起，而且交完还能继续玩。这次的裁决是结构性的——要从根本上改变Google在AI时代的竞争优势。

为什么是现在？AI改变了反垄断的游戏规则

欧盟反垄断盯Google不是一天两天了——搜索、广告、Android，罚了一轮又一轮。但之前的反垄断，针对的都是上一个时代的市场：搜索市场、移动操作系统市场、广告市场。

这次不一样。这次的裁决，核心关键词是AI助手的互操作性。欧盟认为，Google利用Android和搜索的垄断地位，把自己的AI助手（Gemini）预装在每一部Android手机上、放在搜索结果的最顶部，让竞争对手的AI助手根本没有公平竞争的机会。

这个逻辑很关键。以前反垄断查的是「你在这个市场有没有垄断」，现在查的是「你会不会用这个市场的垄断地位，去拿下一个市场的垄断」。AI助手是下一代计算入口——如果Google用Android和搜索的优势，把Gemini的入口占死了，那以后所有的AI服务都得看Google的脸色。

这就是为什么欧盟要抢在AI市场还没完全定型的时候出手——等格局定了再反，就晚了。

Google怎么应对？「合规」但「不认输」

面对欧盟的裁决，Google的回应很官方：「我们将仔细评估并遵守这些要求，但我们也会考虑上诉。」翻译成人话就是：我先按你说的做，但我不服，我要继续打官司。

这是科技巨头应对监管的标准操作：一边合规一边上诉，拖个三五年，说不定市场环境都变了。而且Google有的是办法「软抵制」——你让我开放接口是吧？我开放，但接口做得特别难用、文档写得特别烂、Bug特别多、出了问题没人管。竞争对手接是能接上，但体验差一大截，用户还是会用回Gemini。

"反垄断的历史就是一部猫鼠游戏史——监管出一招，巨头有一百种方法绕过去。但每次绕过去，垄断的形式就变一次。"—— 一位科技政策研究者

但不管怎么说，这对其他AI助手公司来说都是好事。尤其是那些欧洲本土的AI公司——以前它们想碰Android用户，比登天还难。现在至少有了一条通路，哪怕这条路不好走，也比没有强。

更大的意义在于示范效应。欧盟开了这个头，其他国家和地区会不会跟进？英国、日本、韩国，甚至美国自己——如果AI助手的入口被一两家公司垄断，各国监管都不会坐视不管。

AI时代的反垄断，才刚刚开始。Google只是第一个靶子，绝不会是最后一个。Apple、Microsoft、Amazon、Meta……这些手里有「入口」的公司，一个都跑不掉。

明天见。

The EU's antitrust hammer just landed precisely on Google's AI pressure point.

On July 16, the EU issued two new rulings under the Digital Markets Act (DMA): Google must open key parts of Android and Google Search to competitors, allowing rival AI assistants and search engines access. Put simply: your phone runs Android, but you can use a different AI assistant — and Google can't sabotage it.

This isn't a normal fine — Google can pay fines and keep playing. This ruling is structural — it aims to fundamentally change Google's competitive advantage in the AI era.

Why Now? AI Changes the Antitrust Game

EU antitrust hasn't just discovered Google overnight — they've gone after search, ads, Android, fined them round after round. But previous antitrust targeted markets of the previous era: search, mobile OS, advertising.

This time is different. The core keyword of this ruling is AI assistant interoperability. The EU argues that Google is using its dominant position in Android and search to pre-install its own AI assistant (Gemini) on every Android phone and place it at the top of search results — giving competing AI assistants no chance to compete fairly.

This logic is critical. Previously, antitrust investigated "do you have a monopoly in this market." Now it's investigating "are you using your monopoly in this market to secure a monopoly in the next one." AI assistants are the next computing entry point — if Google uses Android and search advantages to lock up the Gemini entry point, then all AI services going forward will have to play by Google's rules.

That's why the EU is acting now, before the AI market fully solidifies — act after the格局 (landscape) is set and it's too late.

How Google Responds: 'Complying' But 'Not Conceding'

Faced with the EU ruling, Google's response was very official: "We will carefully evaluate and comply with these requirements, but we are also considering an appeal." Translated from corporate speak: we'll do what you say for now, but we don't accept it, and we'll keep fighting in court.

This is standard operating procedure for tech giants facing regulation: comply while appealing, drag it out for three to five years, and by then the market environment might have changed. And Google has plenty of ways to "softly resist" — you want open APIs? Fine, we'll open them, but make them especially hard to use, write terrible documentation, fill them with bugs, and have nobody to call when things break. Competitors can connect — but the experience will be so much worse that users will just go back to Gemini.

"The history of antitrust is a history of cat-and-mouse — regulators make one move, and giants have a hundred ways around it. But each time they go around, the form of monopoly changes."— A tech policy researcher

Either way, this is good news for other AI assistant companies. Especially European domestic AI companies — previously, getting access to Android users was harder than climbing to the sky. Now there's at least a path. Even if the path is rough, it's better than nothing.

The bigger significance is the demonstration effect. The EU has set this precedent — will other countries and regions follow? The UK, Japan, Korea, even the US itself — if the AI assistant entry point gets monopolized by one or two companies, no regulator will sit idly by.

AI-era antitrust is just getting started. Google is just the first target — it definitely won't be the last. Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Meta… every company that controls an "entry point" won't escape scrutiny.
