No.005 2026.06.30 约 5 分钟阅读 ~5 min read

MacBook Pro涨500美元，iPad Air涨150美元，Apple TV涨54%，Xbox第三次涨价150美元。

6月25日，苹果和微软在同一天宣布全球硬件涨价。这不是巧合。两家公司的声明中出现了同一个关键词：AI数据中心。

苹果发言人的措辞直白得惊人："AI数据中心的快速扩张造成内存和存储器需求超常规激增，公司从未见过零部件价格涨得如此之快、如此之多。"库克此前在接受《华尔街日报》专访时用了一个更极端的比喻——"百年一遇的洪水"，在超过40年的从业经历中，从未见过类似情况。

消息公布当天，苹果股价收跌6.15%，微软跌3.45%，合计蒸发市值超万亿人民币。市场用脚投票的逻辑很简单：如果连苹果和微软都扛不住成本压力，那AI通胀的影响才刚刚开始。

AI是怎么让你的电脑变贵的

涨价链条清晰得令人窒息。

第一步，五大科技巨头（Alphabet、亚马逊、Meta、微软、Oracle）今年资本支出预计达7410亿美元，同比增长75%，绝大部分砸向AI数据中心。AI服务器对高带宽内存（HBM）的胃口是普通服务器的6-8倍，而HBM产能高度集中在三星、SK海力士和美光三家。

第二步，内存厂商把产能优先供给AI服务器客户——单价高、订单大、付款痛快。Counterpoint Research数据显示，过去三个季度内存和存储价格翻了四倍。美光最新季度毛利率从一年前的39%跃升至84.9%，超过英伟达和Meta，创下历史新高。

第三步，消费电子厂商（苹果、微软、索尼、任天堂）发现自己在内存市场上被AI公司挤到了角落，只能以更高价格争夺剩余产能。Xbox首席执行官Asha Sharma在内部邮件中披露，预计到2027年假日季，微软为存储与内存元件支付的费用将是2024年的五倍。

第四步，成本转嫁给你。MacBook Air从1099美元涨到1299美元（涨18%），16寸MacBook Pro从2499跳到2999美元，iPad Air从599到749美元（涨25%），入门iPad从349到449美元，Apple TV从129到199美元（涨54%）。Xbox Series X将在8月1日迎来第三次涨价，累计涨幅300美元。索尼PlayStation已多次调价，任天堂Switch 2将于9月升至500美元，Valve Steam Machine突破1000美元。

"AI通胀"从论文走进现实

华尔街日报直接将其定性为"AI基建热潮正在制造美国第三波通胀"。

宏观数据已经在印证这个判断。美国劳工部数据显示，今年5月消费级电脑软件及配件价格同比上涨约15%，批发电子元件及配件价格同比飙升27%。高盛预计数据中心将占美国2030年前新增电力需求的近一半，2026-2027年消费者电价将以每年约6%的速度上涨。全美商业经济协会（NABE）的调查显示，81%的受访者认为AI基础设施建设将在未来一年内推高通胀。

这形成了一个讽刺的悖论：AI长期被宣传为"通缩力量"——美联储主席沃什去年11月曾撰文称"AI将提升生产率，每年1个百分点的生产率提升能在一代人时间内使生活水平翻倍"。但瑞银经济学家指出，从当前建设热潮到AI真正压低价格，中间至少还有几年的时间差。在AI的生产率红利兑现之前，普通人先要为AI的建设成本买单。

Counterpoint研究总监Tarun Pathak估计，更高的零部件成本可能使每部iPhone的成本增加约200美元，苹果整体产品线将出现150至200美元的提价。苹果声明中的措辞已经留了后手——"到了需要开始对多款产品提价的时候"。注意"开始"两个字。iPhone没涨，不代表以后不涨。

AI的故事讲了这么久，普通人第一次直接感受到了它的价格——不是在ChatGPT的订阅费里，而是在买电脑、买手机、交电费的账单上。技术革命的成本从来不是均匀分布的，大公司融资建厂的每一分钱，最终都会以某种方式传导到终端。区别只是：之前你没注意到，现在它写在价格标签上了。

MacBook Pro up $500, iPad Air up $150, Apple TV up 54%, Xbox hits its third price hike of $150.

On June 25, Apple and Microsoft announced global hardware price increases on the same day. This wasn't a coincidence. The same keyword appeared in both companies' statements: AI data centers.

Apple's spokesperson was strikingly blunt: "The rapid expansion of AI data centers has caused an extraordinary surge in memory and storage demand; the company has never seen component prices rise this fast and by this much." Tim Cook earlier used an even more extreme metaphor in a Wall Street Journal interview -- a "100-year flood," something he'd never seen in over 40 years in the industry.

The day the news broke, Apple stock closed down 6.15% and Microsoft down 3.45%, with combined market cap losses exceeding 1 trillion RMB. The market's vote-with-feet logic was simple: if even Apple and Microsoft can't absorb cost pressures, AI inflation's impact is only just beginning.

How AI Is Making Your Computer More Expensive

The price hike chain is suffocatingly clear.

Step one: the Big Five tech giants (Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, Microsoft, Oracle) are expected to spend $741 billion in capital expenditure this year, up 75% YoY, overwhelmingly directed at AI data centers. AI servers have 6-8x the appetite for High Bandwidth Memory (HBM) compared to regular servers, and HBM production is highly concentrated in Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron.

Step two: memory makers prioritize capacity for AI server customers -- higher unit prices, bigger orders, faster payment. Counterpoint Research data shows memory and storage prices have quadrupled over the past three quarters. Micron's latest quarter gross margin jumped from 39% a year ago to 84.9%, surpassing Nvidia and Meta to hit an all-time high.

Step three: consumer electronics companies (Apple, Microsoft, Sony, Nintendo) find themselves squeezed to the margins of the memory market by AI companies, forced to compete for remaining capacity at higher prices. Xbox CEO Asha Sharma disclosed in an internal email that by the 2027 holiday season, Microsoft expects to pay 5x what it paid in 2024 for storage and memory components.

Step four: costs get passed to you. MacBook Air goes from $1,099 to $1,299 (up 18%), 16-inch MacBook Pro jumps from $2,499 to $2,999, iPad Air from $599 to $749 (up 25%), entry iPad from $349 to $449, Apple TV from $129 to $199 (up 54%). Xbox Series X gets its third price hike on August 1, bringing cumulative increases to $300. Sony PlayStation has seen multiple price adjustments; Nintendo Switch 2 rises to $500 in September; Valve Steam Machine breaks $1,000.

"AI Inflation" Moves from Academic Papers to Reality

The Wall Street Journal directly characterized it as "the AI infrastructure boom is creating America's third wave of inflation."

Macro data is confirming this assessment. US Department of Labor data shows consumer computer software and accessories prices rose approximately 15% YoY in May, while wholesale electronic components and accessories prices soared 27% YoY. Goldman Sachs projects data centers will account for nearly half of new US electricity demand through 2030, and consumer electricity prices will rise at approximately 6% per year in 2026-2027. A National Association for Business Economics (NABE) survey found 81% of respondents believe AI infrastructure construction will push up inflation within the next year.

This creates an ironic paradox: AI has long been promoted as a "deflationary force" -- Fed Chair Warsh wrote last November that "AI will boost productivity, and a 1 percentage point annual productivity gain can double living standards within a generation." But UBS economists point out that from the current construction boom to AI genuinely driving down prices, there's at least a multi-year lag. Before AI's productivity dividend materializes, ordinary people are footing the bill for AI's construction costs first.

Counterpoint Research Director Tarun Pathak estimates higher component costs could add roughly $200 to the cost of each iPhone, with across-the-board $150-200 price increases across Apple's product lines. Apple's statement already left the door open -- "it's time to start raising prices on multiple products." Note the word "start." iPhones haven't gone up yet, which doesn't mean they won't.

The AI story has been told for so long, and ordinary people are feeling its price directly for the first time -- not in ChatGPT subscription fees, but in the bills for computers, phones, and electricity. The costs of a technological revolution are never evenly distributed; every dollar big companies raise to fund factories eventually reaches end users in some form. The only difference: before you didn't notice, and now it's written on the price tag.
