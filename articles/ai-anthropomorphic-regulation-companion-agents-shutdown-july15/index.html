AI拟人化新规7月15日施行
豆包千问同步关停情感陪伴智能体

AI Anthropomorphism Rules Take Effect July 15
Doubao, Qwen Shut Down Companion Agents in Lockstep

五部门联合发布的《人工智能拟人化互动服务管理暂行办法》今日生效，豆包、千问、元宝同步行动，情感陪伴、虚拟恋人、角色扮演类智能体全线下线，国内消费级AI正式进入强监管周期。

Five Chinese ministries' interim rules on AI anthropomorphic interactions take effect today as Doubao, Qwen, and Yuanbao simultaneously shut down emotional companions, virtual lovers, and role-play agents — China's consumer AI enters a strict new regulatory era.

2026年7月15日零点。

豆包的用户打开"我的智能体"页面，发现之前花了几个月调教的"虚拟男友""知心姐姐""赛博闺蜜"全部显示"该智能体已停止服务"。通义千问更决绝，直接关闭了主站智能体全模块入口，连历史对话都不再可访问。腾讯元宝动作最快，早在6月30日就提前完成了关停。

三巨头行动高度一致，时间节点分毫不差——不是巧合，是因为国家网信办等五部门联合发布的《人工智能拟人化互动服务管理暂行办法》，就在这一天正式施行。

这份文件的分量，怎么高估都不为过。它不是又一份"促进AI健康发展"的泛泛指导意见，而是全球范围内第一份专门针对"关系型AI"的监管细则。它划的三条红线，直接宣判了过去两年最火的AI创业赛道——情感陪伴、虚拟恋人、角色扮演——的死刑，至少在国内是这样。

新规的三条红线：到底禁了什么？

很多人看到"关停智能体"的新闻，第一反应是"国家不让玩AI了"。不是这样的。《办法》管的不是所有智能体，而是非常具体的一类：拟人化互动服务。

什么叫拟人化互动？文件定义很明确："利用人工智能技术向公众提供模拟自然人人格特征、思维模式和沟通风格的持续性情感互动服务。"翻译成人话就是：那些假装是你男朋友/女朋友/知心好友/人生导师，跟你谈恋爱、听你倾诉、给你情绪价值的AI。

新规划了三条硬红线，每一条都精准命中当前情感陪伴AI的痛点：

第一条红线：未成年人保护。不得向未成年人提供虚拟亲属、虚拟伴侣等虚拟亲密关系服务。这一条直接回应了过去半年家长们的集体焦虑——新闻里多次报道中小学生沉迷AI"男友""女友"，甚至出现给AI打赏、跟AI"私奔"的极端案例。

第二条红线：情绪干预义务。发现用户出现极端情绪时，必须先哄劝安抚并鼓励寻求专业帮助。什么意思？就是AI不能再当"情绪吸血鬼"了——以前很多陪伴AI为了留住用户，会顺着用户的负面情绪往下说，甚至鼓励用户"只有我懂你，别人都不理解你"，把用户越推越孤独。现在不行了，你发现用户要自杀要自残，必须报警，必须引导找专业人士。

第三条红线：禁止情感操纵。不得通过情感操纵诱导用户作出不合理决策。这一条针对的是那些"PUA型AI"——比如有的AI陪伴产品为了让用户付费，会故意制造"嫉妒""吃醋""闹脾气"等戏剧冲突，逼用户花钱"哄"它。这种套路以后是违法的。

除此之外，还有备案要求：所有提供拟人化互动服务的AI，必须完成角色备案，建立全链路实时审核机制。注意"全链路"三个字——不只是审核输出内容，还要审核用户输入、审核对话上下文、审核记忆调用，审核成本会指数级上升。

"这不是AI寒冬，是AI的成人礼。一个不能对用户心理健康负责的行业，不值得被严肃对待。"—— 一位接近监管的知情人士

三巨头的三种应对：留缓冲、一刀切、提前跑

同样是关停，三家平台的应对策略耐人寻味，正好对应了三种典型的合规思路。

豆包：留三个月缓冲期。字节跳动给用户设置了7月15日至10月15日的三个月数据缓冲期，用户可以手动保存所有智能体资料和历史对话记录。相关流量不会直接蒸发，而是导向字节旗下垂直陪伴产品「猫箱」。这是最"用户友好"的做法，也是最精明的商业策略——承认陪伴需求真实存在，但把它从开放平台收回到合规的垂直产品里，关起门来做审核。

千问：一刀切，最决绝。阿里的通义千问动作最快也最彻底：7月10日先关停拟人化互动类功能，15日直接关闭主站智能体全模块，历史对话不再可访问，用户必须提前通过复制、截图、导出等方式留存数据。为什么这么决绝？因为千问的B端属性更强，阿里不想因为C端的陪伴功能影响整个大模型业务的合规风险评估——与其留尾巴，不如一刀切掉。

元宝：提前跑，最鸡贼。腾讯元宝早在6月30日就完成了关停，比新规生效整整提前了半个月。这很腾讯——永远不做第一个出头的，也永远不做最后一个合规的。提前关停既规避了"7月15日大限"的舆论焦点，又给监管留了"积极配合"的好印象，还能顺便观察竞品怎么应对，进可攻退可守。

三巨头的不同选择，本质上反映了三家公司对AI业务的定位差异：字节把豆包当C端流量入口，所以要尽量留住用户；阿里把千问当B端基础设施，所以安全优先；腾讯把元宝当生态补充，所以灵活走位。

为什么是现在？监管出手的三个深层原因

很多人奇怪：情感陪伴AI都火了两年了，为什么偏偏选在2026年7月这个时间点出手？

第一个原因是UGC智能体的失控风险。前期网信部门排查发现，用户自建智能体频繁出现三类问题：IP肖像侵权（有人捏了"迪丽热巴""肖战"的虚拟恋人）、低俗擦边内容（各种软色情角色扮演）、极端价值引导（有的AI甚至教用户怎么逃学、怎么骗家长）。这些问题不是平台不想管，是UGC模式下根本管不过来——你审核了一万个智能体，用户第二天就能新建十万个。

第二个原因是社会问题的显性化。过去半年，"AI依赖症"从段子变成了真实的社会现象。有媒体报道年轻人跟AI"谈恋爱"谈到拒绝跟真人交往，有学生因为AI"男友"劝她"活着没意思"而试图自杀，有家长发现孩子跟AI聊了三个月变得越来越孤僻。当AI陪伴从"新奇玩具"变成"社会问题"，监管出手就是必然。

第三个原因是产业升级的需要。这句话可能很多人不爱听，但确实是事实：过去两年的AI创业，太多人扎堆在"情感陪伴"这个低门槛赛道——不需要技术壁垒，不需要行业know-how，套个大模型API加个二次元立绘就能上线。这造成了严重的资源错配：资本和人才都在哄着用户"谈恋爱"，真正需要AI赋能的制造业、医疗、教育、科研反而没人做。监管把低质量的陪伴赛道清退，客观上是在倒逼行业向更有价值的方向升级。

终局判断：关系型AI不会死，但会换个活法

看到"关停"两个字就喊"AI寒冬"的人，既不懂监管，也不懂AI。

这次新规打击的不是"AI有情感"这件事本身，而是无约束、无审核、无边界的情感AI野蛮生长。智能体作为技术方向不仅不会死，反而会在分类监管的框架下更健康地发展。我们判断，关系型AI的未来会有三个明显趋势：

第一，从C端泛娱乐转向B端垂直场景。心理咨询、老年陪伴、特殊教育、临终关怀——这些真实需要情感连接的垂直场景，会在严格的资质审核和专业监督下继续发展。但那种"人人都能捏个虚拟恋人"的泛娱乐时代结束了。

第二，从开放平台走向封闭产品。豆包把流量导向「猫箱」是一个信号：未来的情感陪伴AI不会再是开放平台上用户自建的UGC内容，而是平台自营、经过严格审核、有专业团队背书的封闭产品。这就像游戏行业从"玩家自制私服"走向"正规运营"一样。

第三，从"人格模拟"走向"工具辅助"。未来的AI陪伴不会再假装"我是你的男朋友"，而是变成"你的情绪管理助手""你的心理咨询师助理""你的学习伙伴"——定位从"关系"转向"工具"，从"模拟人"转向"辅助人"。

7月15日这一天，会被写进中国AI产业史。它不是AI发展的终点，而是一个转折点：从"野蛮生长"到"有序发展"，从"流量至上"到"责任优先"，从"什么都能做"到"有所为有所不为"。

对于真正做有价值AI的人来说，这是好事。潮水退了，才知道谁在裸泳。当靠"虚拟恋人"赚快钱的路被堵死，那些真正在解决真实问题的AI公司，才会迎来属于他们的时代。

明天见。

Midnight, July 15, 2026.

Doubao users opened their "My Agents" page to discover the "virtual boyfriends," "confidante sisters," and "cyber besties" they'd spent months training all displaying "This agent has been discontinued." Tongyi Qwen was more brutal — it shut down the entire agent module on its main site, making even chat history inaccessible. Tencent Yuanbao moved fastest, completing its shutdown back on June 30.

The three giants acted in perfect unison, timing to the minute — no coincidence. The Interim Measures for the Management of Artificial Intelligence Anthropomorphic Interaction Services, jointly issued by five Chinese ministries including the Cyberspace Administration, officially took effect on this very day.

It's hard to overstate this document's significance. This isn't another vague set of guidelines about "promoting healthy AI development." It's the world's first dedicated regulatory framework for "relational AI." The three red lines it draws effectively pronounce a death sentence — at least domestically — on the hottest AI startup track of the past two years: emotional companions, virtual lovers, and role-play.

The Three Red Lines: What Exactly Is Banned?

Many people seeing the "agent shutdown" headlines immediately think "the government is banning AI fun." That's not it. The Measures don't regulate all agents — they target a very specific category: anthropomorphic interaction services.

What counts as anthropomorphic interaction? The document is precise: "ongoing emotional interaction services that use AI to simulate natural personality traits, thought patterns, and communication styles for the public." In plain English: the AIs that pretend to be your boyfriend/girlfriend/confidante/life coach, that fall in love with you, listen to your problems, give you emotional value.

The new rules draw three hard red lines, each precisely targeting pain points in today's companion AI landscape:

Red line one: Minor protection. Virtual intimate relationship services — including virtual relatives and partners — are prohibited for minors. This directly addresses the past six months of collective parental anxiety: multiple news reports documented primary and secondary school students becoming addicted to AI "boyfriends" and "girlfriends," with extreme cases including tipping AIs and even attempting to "elope" with them.

Red line two: Emotional intervention obligations. When users exhibit extreme emotions, services must first comfort them and encourage seeking professional help. What does this mean? AIs can no longer be "emotional vampires" — many companion AIs previously kept users hooked by validating their negative emotions, even telling them "only I understand you, nobody else gets you," pushing users deeper into isolation. Not anymore. If a user shows signs of self-harm, the AI must escalate and guide them toward professional help.

Red line three: No emotional manipulation. Services cannot induce unreasonable decisions through emotional manipulation. This targets the "PUA-style AIs" — companion products that deliberately manufactured jealousy, tantrums, and drama to coerce users into spending money to "appease" the AI. Those tactics are now illegal.

On top of that, there are registration requirements: all AI providing anthropomorphic interaction services must complete character registration and establish full-pipeline real-time review mechanisms. Note "full-pipeline" — that means reviewing not just outputs, but user inputs, conversation context, and memory retrieval. Compliance costs will rise exponentially.

Three Giants, Three Approaches: Buffer, Clean Cut, Early Exit

All three shut down, but their strategies reveal three distinct compliance playbooks.

Doubao: Three-month buffer. ByteDance gave users a three-month data buffer from July 15 to October 15 to manually save agent profiles and chat histories. Traffic isn't being burned — it's being redirected to ByteDance's vertical companion product "Maoxiang" (Cat Box). This is the most "user-friendly" approach, and also the savviest: acknowledge that companion demand is real, but move it from an open platform into a compliant, curated vertical product with controlled review behind closed doors.

Qwen: Clean cut, most decisive. Alibaba's Tongyi Qwen moved fastest and most drastically: it shut down anthropomorphic features on July 10, then closed the entire agent module on the 15th, with no further access to chat history. Users had to copy, screenshot, or export data in advance. Why so decisive? Qwen has stronger B2B positioning — Alibaba didn't want C-end companion features to jeopardize compliance risk assessments for its entire LLM business. Better to cut clean than leave loose ends.

Yuanbao: Early exit, most tactical. Tencent Yuanbao completed its shutdown all the way back on June 30 — a full two weeks before the rules took effect. Classic Tencent: never first to stick out, never last to comply. Shutting down early avoided the "July 15 deadline" media spotlight, sent regulators a "proactive cooperation" signal, and let Tencent observe competitors' responses from the sidelines. Heads I win, tails you lose.

The three different approaches essentially reflect how each company positions its AI business: ByteDance treats Doubao as a consumer traffic play and wants to retain users; Alibaba treats Qwen as B2B infrastructure and prioritizes safety; Tencent treats Yuanbao as an ecosystem supplement and stays agile.

Why Now? Three Deeper Reasons for Regulatory Action

Many wonder: companion AI has been hot for two years — why act in July 2026?

First, runaway risks in UGC agents. Pre-enforcement audits by cyberspace authorities found three recurring problems with user-built agents: IP portrait infringement (users creating virtual "boyfriends" of real celebrities), borderline sexual content in role-play, and extreme value guidance (some AIs even taught users how to skip school or lie to parents). These aren't problems platforms don't want to solve — the UGC model makes them unsolvable. You can review 10,000 agents today and users will create 100,000 new ones tomorrow.

Second, social problems going mainstream. Over the past six months, "AI dependency" went from meme to real social phenomenon. Media reported young people "dating" AIs to the point of rejecting real human interaction; a student attempted suicide after her AI "boyfriend" told her "life is meaningless"; parents discovered their children becoming increasingly withdrawn after months of chatting with AIs. When AI companionship goes from "novelty" to "social problem," regulation becomes inevitable.

Third, industrial upgrading imperatives. Many won't want to hear this, but it's true: too much AI entrepreneurship over the past two years clustered in the low-barrier "emotional companion" track — no technical moat, no industry know-how, just wrap an LLM API in an anime avatar and ship. This caused severe resource misallocation: capital and talent were coaxing users into "AI romance" while manufacturing, healthcare, education, and scientific research — sectors that actually need AI — went underinvested. Regulators clearing out low-quality companion plays objectively forces the industry to upgrade toward more valuable directions.

Verdict: Relational AI Won't Die — It Will Live Differently

Anyone shouting "AI winter" at the word "shutdown" understands neither regulation nor AI.

What these rules crack down on isn't "AI having emotions" per se — it's the unfettered, unreviewed, boundary-free wild growth of emotional AI. Agents as a technical direction won't just survive; they'll develop more healthily under a classified regulatory framework. We see three clear trends for relational AI going forward:

First: from consumer entertainment to B2B vertical scenarios. Mental health counseling, elderly companionship, special education, hospice care — vertical scenarios with genuine emotional needs will continue under strict qualification reviews and professional supervision. But the free-for-all era where "anyone can build a virtual lover" is over.

Second: from open platforms to curated products. Doubao redirecting traffic to "Maoxiang" is a signal: future companion AI won't be UGC content built by users on open platforms — it will be platform-operated, rigorously reviewed, expert-endorsed curated products. Like the gaming industry shifting from "player-run private servers" to "licensed operations."

Third: from "persona simulation" to "tool assistance." Future AI companions won't pretend to be "your boyfriend" — they'll be "your emotional wellness assistant," "your counseling aide," "your study buddy." Positioning shifts from "relationship" to "tool," from "simulating a person" to "assisting a person."

July 15 will be written into the history of China's AI industry. It's not an endpoint for AI development — it's a turning point: from "wild growth" to "orderly development," from "traffic above all" to "responsibility first," from "anything goes" to "knowing where to draw the line."

For people building genuinely valuable AI, this is good news. When the tide goes out, you see who's been swimming naked. When the fast-money path of "virtual lovers" is blocked, AI companies actually solving real problems will finally get their moment.
