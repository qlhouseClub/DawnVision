7月21日，韩国《人工智能基本法》实施令及配套法规正式生效。这意味着，全球又一个主要经济体的AI监管框架，从纸面落到了地上。

韩国的AI基本法走的是「双轨制」路线：一条轨管生成式AI的内容标识和透明度，另一条轨管高风险AI系统的安全评估。对普通用户来说，最直观的变化是：以后用韩国本土的AI生成工具做图、写文、剪视频，输出结果上必须有明确的「AI生成」标识——不能再拿AI生成的东西假装是人做的。

韩国版AI监管有什么不一样

如果把全球主要经济体的AI监管框架放在一起对比，你会发现各有各的侧重点。

欧盟AI法案是「风险分级」思路——把AI系统按风险分成四档，不可接受风险的直接禁，高风险的严格管，中低风险的只管透明度。欧盟的逻辑是「先定风险等级，再定监管强度」，体系最完整，但也最复杂。

中国的生成式AI管理办法是「准入+内容」思路——提供生成式AI服务要备案，生成内容要符合社会主义核心价值观，不能有违法违规内容。中国的逻辑是「谁提供服务谁负责」，监管重点在内容治理和服务准入。

美国呢？美国到现在还没有一部联邦层面的综合性AI立法，主要靠打官司来划定边界——版权官司、反垄断官司、隐私官司，一个案子一个案子地磨，磨出一条规则算一条。好处是灵活，坏处是不确定性高，企业不知道红线在哪。

韩国的路子介于欧盟和中国之间。它既有类似欧盟的高风险系统评估制度，也有类似中国的内容治理要求，还加了一些韩国特色——比如要求达到一定规模的AI企业必须设立AI安全负责人，类似于韩国互联网领域已经实行的「信息保护负责人」制度。

具体来说，韩国AI基本法的核心要求包括：生成式AI服务提供者必须对生成内容进行标识；高风险AI系统（医疗、金融、教育、交通等领域）必须进行安全评估并接受政府检查；AI研发企业要建立AI伦理体系；设立国家AI委员会统筹协调；对违反规定的企业可以处以最高营业额3%的罚款。

全球监管竞赛的真实含义

短短一个月内，欧盟AI法案全面生效（7月1日）、韩国AI基本法落地（7月21日），再加上已经实施的中国生成式AI管理办法，全球AI监管的拼图正在快速合拢。

很多人看到AI监管的第一反应是「又要被管了」，但从产业角度看，清晰的监管其实是利好。因为企业最怕的不是被管，而是不知道怎么被管。规则清晰了，企业才能做长期规划，才敢投入资源，资本才敢下注。

但全球监管碎片化也带来了新问题：每个地方的规则不一样，AI公司的合规成本会指数级上升。一个想做全球市场的AI公司，得同时满足欧盟的风险分级、中国的内容审查、韩国的安全负责人、美国各州的隐私法……每进入一个新市场，就要做一遍合规改造。对于大公司来说这是成本，对于小公司来说这可能就是不可逾越的门槛。

"AI是第一个在发明之初就被全球同时监管的技术。以前的技术是先发展后治理，AI是边发展边治理——这本身就是一场实验。"—— Dawn Vision编辑部

不过，监管竞赛也有正面意义。不同国家试不同的监管思路，某种程度上也是在做「监管实验」——哪种模式既能促进创新又能控制风险，几年之后就能看出分晓。到时候做得好的模式会被其他国家借鉴，全球监管标准会慢慢趋同。

韩国AI基本法的生效，是全球AI监管拼图的又一块。它不是最大的一块，也不是最重要的一块，但每多一块，整个框架就更完整一点，产业的预期就更清晰一点。

对于AI公司来说，该来的总会来。与其抱怨监管，不如早点把合规体系建起来——毕竟，能在各种监管框架下都活得好好的公司，才是真正能活到最后的公司。

明天见。

On July 21, South Korea's Framework Act on Artificial Intelligence and its enforcement decree officially took effect. This means AI regulation in yet another major global economy has moved from paper to practice.

Korea's AI framework law follows a "two-track" approach: one track governs content labeling and transparency for generative AI, the other governs safety assessment for high-risk AI systems. For regular users, the most visible change is this: from now on, when you use Korean domestic AI tools to generate images, write text, or edit videos, the output must carry a clear "AI-generated" label — you can't pass AI-generated content off as human-made anymore.

What Makes Korea's AI Regulation Different

If you compare the AI regulatory frameworks of major economies side by side, you'll find each has its own focus.

The EU AI Act follows a "risk-based classification" approach — AI systems are divided into four risk tiers. Unacceptable risk systems are banned outright, high-risk ones are strictly regulated, and medium/low risk ones only need transparency. The EU's logic is "determine risk level first, then regulatory intensity." It's the most comprehensive system, but also the most complex.

China's generative AI management measures follow a "access + content" approach — providing generative AI services requires filing for the record, generated content must align with socialist core values, and no illegal content is allowed. China's logic is "whoever provides the service is responsible," with regulation focused on content governance and service access.

The US? It still doesn't have a comprehensive federal AI law. It mainly draws boundaries through litigation — copyright lawsuits, antitrust cases, privacy cases — grinding out rules one case at a time. The upside is flexibility. The downside is high uncertainty; companies don't know where the red lines are.

Korea's path sits between the EU and China. It has high-risk system assessment similar to the EU, content governance requirements similar to China's, plus some Korean特色 — like requiring AI companies above a certain scale to appoint an AI safety officer, similar to the "information protection officer" system already in place for Korea's internet sector.

Specifically, the core requirements of Korea's AI Framework Act include: generative AI service providers must label generated content; high-risk AI systems (in medical, financial, educational, transportation, etc.) must undergo safety assessment and government inspection; AI R&D companies must establish AI ethics systems; a national AI committee will coordinate overall policy; and violating companies can face fines of up to 3% of revenue.

The Real Meaning of the Global Regulatory Race

In just one month, the EU AI Act went fully into effect (July 1) and Korea's AI Framework Act landed (July 21). Add China's already-implemented generative AI management measures, and the global AI regulatory puzzle is rapidly coming together.

Many people's first reaction to AI regulation is "more red tape," but from an industry perspective, clear regulation is actually good news. Because what companies fear most isn't being regulated — it's not knowing how they'll be regulated. When rules are clear, companies can plan long-term, dare to invest resources, and capital dares to place bets.

But global regulatory fragmentation also creates a new problem: different rules everywhere mean compliance costs for AI companies rise exponentially. An AI company aiming for global markets has to simultaneously satisfy the EU's risk classification, China's content review, Korea's safety officer requirements, US state-level privacy laws… entering each new market means another round of compliance overhauls. For big companies this is a cost; for small companies it might be an insurmountable barrier.

"AI is the first technology that's being regulated globally from the moment of its invention. Previous technologies were developed first and governed later. AI is being developed and governed simultaneously — which is itself an experiment."— The Dawn Vision Editorial Desk

That said, the regulatory race also has positive sides. Different countries testing different regulatory approaches is, in a way, a "regulatory experiment" — which model both promotes innovation and controls risk will become clear in a few years. The best models will then be adopted by other countries, and global regulatory standards will gradually converge.

Korea's AI Framework Act taking effect is another piece of the global AI regulatory puzzle. It's not the biggest piece, nor the most important one, but each additional piece makes the whole framework more complete and industry expectations clearer.

For AI companies, what's coming is coming. Instead of complaining about regulation, it's better to build compliance systems early — because the companies that thrive under various regulatory frameworks are the ones that will truly survive to the end.
